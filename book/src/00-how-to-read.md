# How to Read This Book

This book has five doors. You do not need more than one of them.

If you build machine translation or work with language models, enter
through the question of evaluation: how would you ever know a translation
of a philosophical text is right? The answer here is a working system, and
its architecture (Chapters 9 to 12) transfers.

If you care about theorem provers, enter through the domain: a
formalization target that is not mathematics, with a small decidable
kernel (Chapters 5 to 8) and an exotic, serious source of design ideas.

If you love Sanskrit literature, enter through the text: the complete
first book of the Vākyapadīya, in Devanagari and transliteration, with a
fresh translation and commentary, verse by verse (Part IV).

If you study vyākaraṇa, enter through the tradition: Pāṇini to
Bhartṛhari taken seriously as formal systems (Chapters 3, 4, 8, and 24).

If commentary itself is your subject, enter through the vṛtti: this whole
project treats the commentary as the spine of meaning, and makes every
interpretive ruling explicit, cited, and checkable (Chapters 2, 9, 12, 23).

The chapters form one story and are meant to be read in order, but each
carries only the machinery it needs. Boxed passages marked *For the
engineer*, *For the paṇḍit*, and *In the vṛtti* go deeper for one door at
a time; skipping them never breaks the plot. Each chapter ends with a
short *thread so far*.

Nothing in this book asks you to take a verification claim on trust.
Every theorem mentioned lives in a public repository, rebuilds from
scratch, and is indexed in Appendix D.
