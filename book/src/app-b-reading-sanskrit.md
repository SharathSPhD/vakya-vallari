# Appendix B · Reading and Saying Sanskrit

This appendix is a working guide, not a linguistics course. It gives the full letter tables, plain descriptions of the sounds, and one verse to practice on. It also serves as the narrator's guide for the audiobook edition; the notes in that section are instructions, not suggestions. The front matter's *A Note on Sanskrit* named the two habits that repay effort immediately: long vowels take twice the time of short ones, and the retroflex consonants curl the tongue back. Everything below builds on those two.

## How the script works

Devanagari is not an alphabet in the English sense. It is an alphasyllabary: the unit of writing is the syllable, and the letters are built accordingly. Four rules generate almost everything you will see.

First, every consonant letter carries an inherent vowel. The letter क is not "k" but *ka*. A bare row of consonant letters is already pronounceable.

Second, any other vowel attaches to the consonant as a sign: कि *ki*, की *kī*, कु *ku*, कू *kū*, के *ke*, को *ko*. The sign may sit after, above, or below its consonant, and the sign for short *i* (as in कि) is written before the consonant but pronounced after it. The reading order is always consonant first, vowel second. A vowel with no consonant of its own, at the start of a word for instance, uses its full independent letter: अ, आ, इ, and so on down the table below.

Third, the virāma, a small stroke beneath the letter, kills the inherent vowel: क् is bare *k*. It appears mostly at the ends of verse lines and in citation forms.

Fourth, when consonants cluster with no vowel between them, their letters merge into a conjunct: क plus त gives क्त *kta*; the cluster in *śabdatattva* is त्त्व *ttva*. Some conjuncts stack vertically, some fuse side by side, and a few, such as क्ष *kṣa* and ज्ञ *jña*, are ligatures one simply learns. Conjuncts are the only genuinely hard part of the script, and the transliteration removes the difficulty: IAST spells every cluster out, one sound per letter, in order.

Two small signs complete the kit and appear in the vowel table below: the anusvāra, a dot above the line (ं, IAST *ṃ*), marks nasality, and the visarga, two dots after the syllable (ः, IAST *ḥ*), marks a light breath.

## The vowels

Sanskrit vowels come in short and long pairs. Long vowels are not louder or stressed; they are simply held twice as long, two beats to the short vowel's one. The vowels *e*, *ai*, *o*, *au* have no short partners and always take two beats, even though IAST writes *e* and *o* without a length mark.

| Devanagari | IAST | Say it like |
|---|---|---|
| अ | a | the "u" in English *but*; short and relaxed |
| आ | ā | the "a" in *father*; hold it two beats |
| इ | i | the "i" in *bit* |
| ई | ī | the "ee" in *seen*; two beats |
| उ | u | the "u" in *put* |
| ऊ | ū | the "oo" in *boot*; two beats |
| ऋ | ṛ | a vowel-like *r*; "ri" with a tapped r is acceptable |
| ॠ | ṝ | the same sound held two beats; rare |
| ऌ | ḷ | a vowel-like *l*, roughly the "le" of *table*; vanishingly rare |
| ए | e | the "ay" in *say* without the closing glide; always two beats |
| ऐ | ai | the "ai" in *aisle*; two beats |
| ओ | o | the "o" in *go* without the closing glide; always two beats |
| औ | au | the "ou" in *loud*; two beats |
| अं | aṃ | anusvāra: a nasal close to the vowel; at the end of a word, say "m" |
| अः | aḥ | visarga: a light breath faintly echoing the vowel, *aḥ* like a whispered "aha" |

## The consonants

The tradition does not order its consonants alphabetically. It orders them anatomically, by where in the mouth they are made, moving from the throat outward to the lips: velar (back of the tongue at the soft palate), palatal (tongue body at the hard palate), retroflex (tongue tip curled back), dental (tongue tip at the teeth), labial (the lips). Within each row the same five-way pattern repeats: unvoiced, unvoiced with aspiration, voiced, voiced with aspiration, nasal. Aspiration means an extra puff of breath, and in Sanskrit it is a separate sound, not a shade of the same one: *kh* is *k* plus an audible puff, *bh* is *b* plus a puff, and swapping them changes words.

| | unvoiced | unvoiced aspirate | voiced | voiced aspirate | nasal |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| velar | क k | ख kh | ग g | घ gh | ङ ṅ |
| palatal | च c | छ ch | ज j | झ jh | ञ ñ |
| retroflex | ट ṭ | ठ ṭh | ड ḍ | ढ ḍh | ण ṇ |
| dental | त t | थ th | द d | ध dh | न n |
| labial | प p | फ ph | ब b | भ bh | म m |

Honest hints, row by row. The unaspirated unvoiced stops are the "clean" versions English uses after *s*: *k* as in *skip*, *t* as in *stop*, *p* as in *spin*. The letter *c* is the English "ch" of *church* minus the puff of breath; it is never the "k" of *cat* and never the "s" of *cell*. The retroflex row is made with the tongue tip curled back to touch the roof of the mouth; an American "t" in *hurt* is in the neighborhood. The dental row puts the tongue tip against the upper teeth, as in Spanish or French *t* and *d*, further forward than English. Note that *ph* is *p* with breath, never the "f" of *photo*, and *th* is *t* with breath, never the "th" of *think*. The nasals follow their rows: ङ *ṅ* is the "ng" of *sing*, ञ *ñ* the "ny" of *canyon*, ण *ṇ* a nasal with the tongue curled back, न *n* and म *m* as in English.

After the grid come four semivowels, three sibilants, and *h*:

| Devanagari | IAST | Say it like |
|---|---|---|
| य | y | *yes* |
| र | r | a tapped r, as in Spanish *pero* |
| ल | l | *love* |
| व | v | between English "v" and "w"; a soft *v* |
| श | ś | the "sh" of *ship* |
| ष | ṣ | "sh" with the tongue curled back; near ś is acceptable |
| स | s | *sun* |
| ह | h | *house* |

The two "sh" sounds, palatal *ś* and retroflex *ṣ*, are distinct to a trained ear; a reader who renders both as English "sh" commits a venial sin, not a mortal one.

## Say it aloud

Here is the verse the whole book turns on, VP 1.1, first as printed, then broken into syllables.

::: {.deva}
अनादिनिधनं ब्रह्म शब्दतत्त्वं यदक्षरम् ।

विवर्ततेऽर्थभावेन प्रक्रिया जगतो यतः ॥ १ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
a-nā-di-ni-dha-naṃ brah-ma śab-da-tat-tvaṃ ya-dak-ṣa-ram

vi-var-ta-ter-tha-bhā-ve-na pra-kri-yā ja-ga-to ya-taḥ
:::

Read it with even, stress-neutral syllables: Sanskrit recitation does not punch one syllable per word the way English does. Give every syllable the same weight and let vowel length alone carry the rhythm, with *nā*, *bhā*, *yā*, and the *e* and *o* vowels held two beats. In *dha* and *bhā*, sound the puff of breath. In *dak-ṣa*, the cluster *kṣ* is "k" gliding straight into curled-back "sh". The apostrophe printed in *vivartate'rthabhāvena* is the avagraha, marking an *a* swallowed by sandhi; do not restore it, just read straight through, *te* into *rtha*. The single and double bars (। ॥) are verse punctuation: pause at the first, stop at the second.

## Narrator's notes

These rules govern the audiobook recording.

1. IAST is the reading text. Wherever the page shows Devanagari with an IAST sibling, the narrator reads the IAST. Devanagari passages are never read as script names, never spelled out letter by letter, and never announced ("in Devanagari script..."); the script is silent ink for the listener.
2. Verses are read twice: first the IAST recitation, at an even pace with long vowels at two beats, then the English translation. Nothing is interposed between the two.
3. The schwa is never inserted. English habits pad consonant clusters with a faint "uh" (*suh-photo* for *sphoṭa*); resist it. Clusters are read as written, and every written final *a* is pronounced.
4. ṛ as "ri" with a tapped r is acceptable throughout: *Bhartṛhari* as "Bhar-tri-ha-ri".
5. Aspirates keep their puff and plain stops stay plain, within the narrator's ability; vowel length is the priority when a choice must be made.
6. Sanskrit syllables carry no English word stress. Even weight, length from the vowels.

## Common mistakes

- **Aspirating everything.** English puffs its k, t, p at the start of words by default, which erases Sanskrit's four-way contrast. Borrow the clean stops from *skip*, *stop*, *spin*.
- **Misreading c.** IAST *c* is the unaspirated "ch" of *church*, never "k", never "s". *Vācaspati* begins "vaa-cha", not "vaa-ka".
- **Ignoring vowel length.** Length is meaningful, not decorative; collapsing ā to a can change one word into another. Two beats for ā, ī, ū, e, ai, o, au.
- **Reading ph and th as English.** *Ph* is a breathy p, not "f"; *th* is a breathy t, not the sound in *think*.
- **Dropping the final a.** Hindi drops it; Sanskrit does not. *Sphoṭa* has two full syllables.
- **Stressing like English.** No syllable is punched. The rhythm lives in the long vowels.

That is the whole apparatus. Ten minutes with the tables and one slow pass through the verse above will carry a reader, or a narrator, through everything this book asks aloud.
