The critique of inference cleared the ground. Now the text turns to the thing it has been defending all along: the word itself. This movement opens the sphoṭa doctrine, the core of the Brahmakāṇḍa and the reason Bhartṛhari is read at all. It is the longest stretch so far, twenty-five units, and it begins with a distinction that sounds like a paradox. In every uttered word there are dvau śabdau, two words: one is the cause of words, the other is what is used to mean (1.44).

That distinction is the whole movement in seed. One word is inner, resident in the intellect, whole and unsounded. The other is the sequence of audible sounds. The section's task is to say how the two are related without collapsing them and without splitting them. It does this through a series of similes, each aimed at a specific difficulty. Fire lies latent in the kindling-wood and, when churned, becomes the flame that lights other things: so the word in the intellect is, separately, the cause of audible sounds (1.46). The sound is born in sequence, but the word is neither prior nor posterior; the sequenceless takes on the form of sequence and is born as though divided (1.48). A reflection seems to follow the movements of the water it stands in: so the word seems to take on the length, speed, and accent that belong only to its sounds (1.49). Then the movement broadens from the word's manifestation to its two powers, the power to be grasped and the power to grasp, standing distinct as they do in light (1.55), and finally descends into the machine-room of Pāṇini, where the same two-word structure is shown at work in how a grammatical rule names its own terms (1.59 to 1.69).

Readers arriving from the limits of inference hold a defended authority and an unexamined object. What this movement adds is the object: an account of what a word is. The word to carry forward is vivarta, appearance without loss of unity, set against pariṇāma, real transformation. The whole doctrine hangs on the difference. The word does not turn into its sounds the way milk turns into curd. It stays whole in the speaker while the sounds manifest it. That is the claim the movement settles, and it hands forward a question it has only begun to face: if the word is one and partless, what are we to make of phonemes, and of the sentence? The next chapter takes up the unity question directly.

Several cards reward a slow read. Slow down at 1.44, because the whole doctrine rests on its deliberate paradox, two words in every word, and the rejected reading fails simply by erasing the distinction. Slow down at 1.48, because this is the metaphysical core, and its vocabulary locks onto the vocabulary of 1.1: akrama, sequenceless, becoming bhedavān iva, as though divided. Slow down at 1.49, the subtlest of the similes, because it answers a precise worry, why the word appears to have temporal properties it does not have, and it answers with a reflection that borrows the water's motion without owning it. And slow down at 1.55, because the two-powers doctrine, grāhyatva and grāhakatva, is where the section stops being about production and starts being about how a word can reveal a meaning and itself at once, the way a lamp is both seen and seen by.

In the cards, watch the divide between pariṇāma and vivarta run through nearly every refutation. Again and again a rejected reading asserts that the word transforms into sound, or is produced by sound from scratch, and the card comes back contradicted, because the commentary denies transformation in so many words: the flame was in the wood in potentia, not created by the churning. A second failure mode runs alongside it: demotion, the sort error that renders śabda as a property, a linguistic structure, rather than the constant principle the similes require. Those come back unlicensed. The registry term to track is sphoṭa, and one homonym deserves special attention. In this neighborhood, and here almost alone, śabda names not a word-item but the Absolute itself; the featured verse depends on that second registered sense, without which its contract would collide with every ordinary śabda contract in the corpus.

Watch the contested boxes, because this is the thickest cluster in the walk so far. Seven cards carry them. Some flag a genuinely open construal of a simile: which of the two words is the cause of words (1.44), what the elliptical compound at 1.49 picks out, whether kratu at 1.51 is energy or literal sacrifice, what the painter's triad at 1.52 refers to. Others flag a doxographic uncertainty: the identity of the purāṇagas whose view 1.45 reports is simply unrecoverable, and the Pāṇinian context 1.60 targets is not named in the verse. One case is worth watching especially. At 1.52 the edition first read the painter simile as a craft constraint, then came to prefer the traditional reading, the word's permanent oscillation between unity and sequence, and it records both, telling you which it now favours and why. That is the apparatus showing its own second thoughts.

One last orientation note. The featured verse of this chapter is 1.46, the fire-in-the-kindling verse, the first of the great similes. The walkthrough that follows this intro is really about the registry: how a single homonym entry, śabda as the Absolute, keeps this one contract from colliding with all the others. The cards follow. The unity of the word, asserted here, becomes the open question of the next chapter.
