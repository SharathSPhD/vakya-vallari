For six chapters the walk has been building. The sphoṭa doctrine was raised, the phoneme, word, and sentence relations were tested, and each verse licensed some readings and refuted others. This movement, 1.102 to 1.122, does something the walk has not done before. It steps back and reports. From 1.102 the kārikās turn doxographic. They survey what rival schools have said about how a sound is made and what, at bottom, becomes the word. Bhartṛhari does not argue here so much as catalogue. The work of the movement is to set the competing accounts side by side, let each state its physics in its own terms, and only at the very end fold the whole survey back into his own cosmology.

The report comes in three panels. The first, 1.102 to 1.106, belongs to the phoneticians. In their usage sphoṭa names the first-produced sound, and the dhvanis are the sounds born from it. Around that naming the panel builds a small physics: the time of the sphoṭa does not vary with loudness though the after-train does (1.103), the bare sound carries from afar like a lamp's glow even where the word is not understood (1.104), the striking of the substance apportions even vowel-length (1.105), and one sound kindles the next as flame from flame (1.106). The second panel, 1.107 to 1.111, widens the question to first principles: by some it is air, by some the atoms, by some cognition that is held to become word, and the divergence settles nowhere (1.107). The air doctrine gets a full mechanist's statement (1.108 to 1.109), and the atomist refinement answers it, its word-atoms gathering like clouds that have edges only at a distance and no oneness for the hearer's single grasp (1.110 to 1.111). The third panel changes temperature. At 1.112 the anonymous reportage gives way to vivarta vocabulary that is Bhartṛhari's own, and the doxography dissolves into the doctrine. The descent of speech then runs through breath, mind, and fire down into the phonemes (1.115), behind which stands a source that is never spent (1.116); 1.117 gathers the stations into one power, 1.118 folds the linguistics back into the cosmology, and after arguments from music, scripture, and the cradle (1.119 to 1.121), 1.122 seals the circle by making even the motor acts of speech depend on a conceiving in word.

Readers arriving here hold a tested instrument and a long stretch of doctrine already walked. What this movement adds is a hazard. It is the most quoted-but-not-endorsed passage in the kāṇḍa, and the standing danger, for a human reader and for a machine alike, is to take the reportage for the thesis. The apparatus meets that danger with a registry ruling, which the featured section on 1.102 lays out. For the span 1.102 to 1.106, sphoṭa is quarantined to its phonetician sense, the first-produced sound, acoustic and produced. It is not allowed to mean the partless meaning-bearer of Bhartṛhari's own doctrine. One term carries two sorts, held apart by the registry so that the report can never contaminate the teaching. Both sides of that wall keep their theorems green.

Four cards reward a slow read. Slow down at 1.104, because it is the panel's one appeal to plain experience: a bell struck nearby shows the difference the theory needs, and the sound of a word reaches the ear from a distance at which its meaning does not. Slow down at 1.107, because a single line, that the divergence of views is unsettled, is the whole doxography's honest hinge, and the rejected reading that commits to air as the sole substrate fails against it. Slow down at 1.112 and 1.113, because this is where the survey stops being a survey: the inner knower turns itself into wordhood by vivarta, not by real change, and the vocabulary that was borrowed from opponents is now unmistakably the author's. And slow down at 1.118, because it is the crescendo, where the power residing in words is named as what binds the universe, and the undivided self is said only to appear in the form of difference.

In the cards, watch the verdict split. The commonest failure mode across the physics panels is reversal. Again and again a rejected reading turns the acoustic relation around: it makes the sphoṭa-time vary while the after-train stays fixed (1.103), assigns vowel-length to the free decay rather than the driven phase (1.105), or makes the organ, not the source, the ongoing producer (1.106). Each such reading contradicts a claim the commentary states outright, so it fails as contradicted, not merely unlicensed. The other pole is the sort error. At 1.114 a reading treats vāyu as ordinary physical substance, a manifestation-sort thing, when the passage means a subtle power, and that mismatch fails as unlicensed. At 1.120 a reading takes parināma, real transformation, as Bhartṛhari's own position rather than as scriptural shorthand for what is strictly vivarta, and fails as contradicted. Two verdicts, two kinds of wrongness, and this chapter drills both.

Watch the contested boxes above all, because this chapter carries more of them than any other in the walk. Nearly every card has one, and they cluster on a single seam: how far the reported terminology of 1.102 still governs the verse in hand. Is the sphoṭa-time of 1.103 the duration of a produced sound, or a restatement of the invariance thesis from 1.75? Does the flame simile of 1.106 fit production or manifestation? Do 1.112 and 1.113 still belong to the doxography of 1.107, or do they mark the author resuming his own voice? The edition does not paper over these seams. It marks each one, states which side it took, and records the doubt. That habit of visible doubt is the part of the apparatus most translations leave out, and here it does its heaviest work.

The report ends by turning inward. Having surveyed how sound is made, the text asks next what cognition is made of, and finds the word already inside it. That is the thesis of the chapter to come.
