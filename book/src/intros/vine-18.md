The sphoṭa doctrine established the word as one and partless, manifested by sounds that unfold in time. That leaves a question the previous movement raised but did not answer: if the word is partless, what are phonemes, and what is a sentence? This movement takes up the unity question head on. Verses 1.70 to 1.101 ask which of the three, phoneme, word, or sentence, is the real linguistic unit, and how a timeless unity can be grasped through sounds that come one after another. It is the longest stretch in the walk, thirty-two units, and it is also the most openly doxographic.

The movement opens with a grid, not a thesis. Theorists divide over unity and plurality, and independently over whether words are produced or eternal (1.70). Then it lays out the two extreme positions and refuses both. On one side, there is no word over and above the phonemes, and no sentence apart from phonemes and words (1.72). On the other, the phonemes do not exist in the word at all, and the word cannot be absolutely separated out of the sentence (1.73). The text declines to referee: practice goes on by differing views, and what is primary for one school is secondary for another (1.74). Its own resolution is not to pick a unit but to relocate the problem. The sphoṭa is abhinna-kāla, without temporal division, yet it appears to follow the time of the sounds that manifest it, so what varies is not the word but its vṛtti, its mode of functioning, according to the conditions of apprehension (1.75). From there the movement works out the consequences: how sounds prepare the hearer (1.78 to 1.81), how a partless word is grasped through a ripening series of sounds (1.82 to 1.85), and how the appearance of parts is a productive error rather than a fact about the word (1.85, 1.90).

Readers arriving from the sphoṭa core hold a word that is one but is heard in sequence. What this movement adds is the reconciliation of the one and the sequence, and it does so with a distinction that will govern the rest of the kāṇḍa: the difference between a thing's own time and the time of its manifestation. The word has no real duration. The duration belongs to the nāda, the sound; the word's short, long, and prolated forms are attributed to it only by upacāra, figurative transfer (1.76). That is the claim the movement settles, stated as a conclusion at its close: for phonemes, words, and sentences alike, whose time is undivided, the time of functioning and the word's own time are distinguished only according to the difference of the sound (1.101). The next chapter hands the physical side of this over to the phoneticians.

Several cards reward a slow read. Slow down at 1.72 and 1.73 as a pair, because they state the two positions the whole movement is built to hold apart, the word as nothing but its phonemes and the phonemes as not existing in the word, and the text endorses neither as its final voice. Slow down at 1.74, because it is where the antinomy is declined rather than resolved, and the commentary's phrase for the loser is exact: the part-primary view is not refuted, only outweighed. Slow down at 1.85, the boldest verse in the stretch, because it diagnoses the appearance of phonemic parts as the apprehender's own incapacity and then declares that very incapacity to be the means of grasping. And slow down at 1.94, because it gives the manifestation relation its cleanest image, the changing sound revealing the changeless word the way a light reveals without infecting, under the commentary's warning not to put the flicker into the pot.

In the cards, watch one failure mode above all: temporalizing the sphoṭa. The rejected readings keep assigning real time to what has none. The sphoṭa is temporally divisible (1.75); the eternal word has real short and long forms (1.76); the modified sounds divide the word's essence (1.77); the sound's modifications transfer to the word (1.94); linguistic units possess distinct time-properties of their own (1.101). Each of these comes back contradicted or unlicensed on the same ground, that duration is a property of nāda and only figuratively of the word. A second failure mode runs beside it: mereology, the reading that composes the word out of phonemes as parts, which the commentary rejects in favour of a hierarchy of alternative unities rather than containment (1.88, 1.97). The registry terms doing the work are sphoṭa, dhvani, nāda, and vṛtti, with upacāra, figurative attribution, as the hinge that lets a timeless word wear a temporal appearance without becoming temporal.

Watch the contested boxes, because they cluster around one recurring doubt: whose voice is speaking. Several cards flag it. Is 1.72 pure pūrvapakṣa, an opponent's view to be superseded, or a moment Bhartṛhari endorses at the level of manifested sound (the verse carries no iti to mark it as another's)? Does 1.74's viparyaya mean the reverse view or an outright error? Does 1.80 endorse the both-preparation position or merely finish the doxography? Is the systematization at 1.93 a view he holds, tolerates, or only catalogues? One box flags a different kind of trouble: at 1.88 the source transcription opens with a corrupt sequence that yields no construable Sanskrit, and the edition translates the evident text and notes the corruption rather than silently repairing it. Taken together, these boxes tell you that this movement reports more than it asserts, and that reading it well means keeping track of which sentences are the text's own.

One last orientation note. The featured verse of this chapter is 1.75, the verse that gives the reflection-simile of 1.49 its doctrinal statement. The walkthrough that follows shows how the contract keeps two entities apart, the timeless sphoṭa and the sequential sounds, so that a mistranslation asserting sequence lands on the wrong entity and fails. The cards follow. From the word's time, the walk moves next to the physics of its sound.
