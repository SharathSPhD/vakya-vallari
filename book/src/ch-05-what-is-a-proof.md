# 5 · What Is a Proof, Really?

You know the Pythagorean theorem. In a right triangle, the square on the longest side equals the sum of the squares on the other two. You have known it since school. Almost certainly you believe it.

Now ask the harder question. How do you know it is true?

For most of us, the honest answer is: somebody told me. A teacher said it, a textbook printed it, and every calculator and carpenter since has agreed. That is not nothing. But it is trust, not knowledge. If you press further, you may remember that there was a proof. Perhaps you even remember the picture: squares drawn on the three sides, areas cut up and rearranged. But do you remember the proof well enough to defend it? If a clever skeptic challenged one step, could you answer? And if you can defend every step, one more turn of the screw: how do you know your defense is sound? How do you know that you know?

This regress has a stopping point, and finding it is one of the oldest achievements of human civilization. The stopping point is called proof. A proof is not a feeling of certainty and it is not a vote of experts. It is a device. It is a social technology whose job is to transfer certainty from one mind to another without asking for trust. The person who checks a proof does not have to believe the person who wrote it. She only has to follow the steps.

This chapter tells the story of that technology, because Part II of this book depends on it and Part I secretly rehearsed it. The story has a single plot: proof lives on a carrier, and every carrier decays. Memory fades. Manuscripts corrupt. Intuitions mislead. Long arguments hide gaps. The history of proof is a search for carriers that do not decay, and it passes through four stations: memory, text, logic, machine. By the end of the chapter the fourth station will be in view, and something unexpected will happen: the first station will turn out to have been more sophisticated than anyone gave it credit for.

## Certainty by construction

Around 300 BCE, in Alexandria, Euclid assembled a book called the Elements. Almost nothing is known about the man. The book, on the other hand, became one of the most copied, translated, and taught texts in history, and it earned that place with a single structural idea.

Euclid begins by laying his cards on the table. He states definitions: a point is that which has no part, a line is breadthless length. He states postulates: between any two points a straight line can be drawn, all right angles are equal, and so on, five in all. He states common notions: things equal to the same thing are equal to one another. And then he stops asking for anything. Every one of the hundreds of propositions that follow is built from the postulates, the common notions, and propositions already established, by steps the reader can inspect one at a time. The Pythagorean theorem appears as Proposition 47 of Book I, and by the time you reach it, every plank under it has been laid in front of you.

This is certainty by construction. The reader of the Elements is never asked to trust Euclid. She is asked to replay him. The proof is not an oracle's pronouncement; it is a machine made of sentences, and it runs the same way every time, for every reader, in every century. A student in medieval Baghdad, a Jesuit in Ming-dynasty China, a schoolchild in Victorian England: each could load the same argument and watch it produce the same certainty. That is the technology. A proof is a replayable object.

Notice what this design buys. The axioms are few and plain, so disagreement is pushed to a small, visible surface: if you accept these five postulates, you must accept everything downstream. The steps are small, so error has nowhere to hide. And the whole structure is written down, so it survives its author. Euclid solved the decay of memory with text and solved the decay of trust with structure.

For over two thousand years, this looked like the end of the story. It was not. The Elements had two hidden weaknesses, and the nineteenth century found both.

## When intuition broke

The first weakness was that Euclid's replayable machine quietly ran on a fuel he never declared: spatial intuition. His proofs lean on diagrams. Steps that look obvious in the figure, such as the claim that a line through the center of a circle meets the circle, are never justified from the axioms, because no reader before the modern era felt they needed to be. The axioms were the visible foundation; intuition was the invisible one.

For as long as mathematics stayed close to the world of figures, the invisible foundation held. In the nineteenth century, mathematics left that world, and the foundation cracked in two places at once.

The first crack ran through analysis, the mathematics of the infinite that had grown out of calculus. Mathematicians studying heat flow and vibrating strings found themselves summing infinite series of functions, and the sums behaved badly. Arguments that felt obviously right gave wrong answers. Functions appeared that no picture could capture: Weierstrass stunned his colleagues with a curve that is continuous everywhere yet has a well-defined direction nowhere. It is all corner, at every point, at every magnification. No hand can draw it and no intuition can vouch for it. If such a thing exists, then "I can see it" is not evidence. Analysis had outgrown the eye.

The second crack was stranger. For centuries, mathematicians had been bothered by Euclid's fifth postulate, the one about parallel lines, which reads less like a self-evident truth than a theorem that lost its proof. Many tried to derive it from the other axioms. In the early nineteenth century, Lobachevsky and Bolyai, with Gauss working privately along the same lines, did something more radical: they denied it and followed the consequences. The expected contradiction never came. Instead a new geometry unfolded, coherent and internally consistent, in which parallels behave differently and the angles of a triangle sum to less than two right angles. Euclid's axioms, it turned out, were not the necessary truths of space. They were one consistent choice among several.

Take the two cracks together and the lesson is severe. Intuition can affirm false things and can fail to see true things, and even the axioms themselves are not given by nature. Nothing may be smuggled. Every assumption must be stated; every step must be justified from stated assumptions and nothing else. This demand, pressed by Weierstrass in analysis, by Peano and Frege in logic, and by Hilbert in geometry, is what mathematicians call rigor. Hilbert's own reworking of Euclid filled the ancient gaps with explicit axioms, and he expressed the new austerity in a famous thought: a geometry proof should still be valid if the words point, line, and plane were replaced throughout by tables, chairs, and beer mugs. Meaning must carry no load. Only stated structure may.

Then, at the turn of the twentieth century, the crisis reached the foundations themselves. The new set theory, the very language in which rigorous mathematics was being rebuilt, was found to permit contradictions. Russell's paradox, the set of all sets that do not contain themselves, showed that even careful-looking axioms could destroy themselves. The question was no longer academic. What, exactly, keeps any of this standing?

## Hilbert's wager

David Hilbert, the leading mathematician of his generation, proposed an answer of magnificent ambition. First, formalize: rebuild all of mathematics inside a fully explicit symbolic system, in which a proof is nothing but a finite sequence of symbol strings, each following from earlier ones by a small fixed set of mechanical rules. In such a system, checking a proof requires no genius and no intuition. It is bookkeeping. A patient clerk, or a machine, could verify any proof by inspection, symbol by symbol.

Second, secure the foundation: prove, using only the most elementary and uncontroversial reasoning, that the grand formal system can never derive a contradiction. Do that once, and the crisis ends permanently. Every theorem ever proved in the system, and every theorem anyone would ever prove in it, would be guaranteed safe, not by the authority of experts but by a single finished piece of mathematics. This program occupied some of the best minds of the 1920s. It is one of the most consequential wagers in the history of thought, and it lost. But the way it lost matters enormously, and it is widely misreported.

## What Gödel actually said

In 1931, Kurt Gödel, then a young logician in Vienna, proved two theorems about formal systems. Stated plainly: first, any consistent formal system rich enough to express ordinary arithmetic must leave some true statements of arithmetic unprovable within the system. Second, no such system can prove its own consistency. The second theorem struck Hilbert's program directly. The elementary reasoning Hilbert wanted to use for the consistency proof lives inside the systems it was supposed to certify, so it cannot do the certifying.

Because these results are deep and their popularizations are shallow, it is worth being precise about what they do not say. They do not say that mathematics is broken, or inconsistent, or a matter of opinion. They do not say that truth is beyond logic or that the mind mystically exceeds every machine. They do not cast the slightest doubt on the Pythagorean theorem or on any ordinary piece of mathematics, all of which is proved from remarkably modest axioms that mathematicians have examined for a century without finding strain. What the theorems say is narrower and cleaner: there is no single, final, self-certifying formal system that contains all of mathematics. Completeness and self-guaranteed safety are not both on the menu. You must adopt axioms, and your reasons for trusting them will live partly outside the system they define.

And here is the point that matters for the rest of this book. Half of Hilbert's program did not merely survive Gödel; Gödel's own proof depends on it. To reason mathematically about proofs, Gödel had to treat them exactly as Hilbert proposed: as finite, formal, mechanically checkable objects. That treatment worked flawlessly. Proof checking is mechanical. Whether a given text is a correct proof from given axioms is a question a dumb procedure can settle with a yes or a no. What died in 1931 was the dream of a final system. What was confirmed in 1931 is the possibility on which every proof assistant now runs.

## The proof nobody writes

If proofs can be fully formal, why did mathematicians not start writing them that way? Because the cost was absurd. The great monument to full formality before computers is Principia Mathematica, the three volumes in which Whitehead and Russell rebuilt mathematics from bare logic between 1910 and 1913. Working at that level of explicitness, they reach the proof that one plus one equals two only well into the work, hundreds of pages from the start, with the dry remark that the proposition is occasionally useful. No mathematician can spend a career at that altitude of detail. No reader could survey the result if they did.

So working mathematics settled into a compromise that persists today. A published proof is not a formal derivation. It is a compressed argument addressed to a trained reader: a route map with the routine terrain left as an exercise. Its correctness is underwritten socially, by referees, seminars, graduate students working through the details, and rival experts with every incentive to find the flaw. The implicit warranty behind a published proof is that a competent reader could, in principle, expand every step down to the formal bedrock. Nobody actually does the expansion. Everybody relies on the fact that somebody could.

This system is one of the most successful quality-control regimes ever built, and it is worth saying so plainly. But its guarantee is statistical, not absolute, and its failure modes are known. Compression hides gaps. Referees are human, and long proofs exhaust them. Published proofs are sometimes found, years later, to contain errors, most repairable, a few fatal. And the system scales badly: as frontier proofs grew from tens of pages to hundreds, the number of people able to survey any one of them shrank toward a handful. The carrier was decaying again, not through corruption of the text but through the thinning of the community able to replay it. Then came a proof that no community could replay at all.

## Four colors and a crisis of trust

In 1852, a student coloring a map of the counties of England noticed that four colors sufficed to keep every pair of neighboring counties distinct, and asked whether four colors suffice for every map. The question, so innocent a child can grasp it, defeated mathematicians for over a century. Purported proofs rose and fell; one stood for a decade before its flaw was found.

In 1976, Kenneth Appel and Wolfgang Haken of the University of Illinois announced a proof, and it was a proof of a new species. Their strategy reduced the problem to checking an enormous but finite family of map configurations, nearly two thousand of them, each requiring an intricate combinatorial analysis. No human could perform those checks in a lifetime. A computer performed them, consuming on the order of a thousand hours of machine time. The theorem was, by every prior standard of the subject, settled.

Many mathematicians refused to be settled. Their unease deserves to be stated carefully, because it was not superstition about machines. Ever since Euclid, a proof had been a replayable object: the certainty it transferred came precisely from the reader's ability to traverse every step. The Appel and Haken proof broke that contract. Its crucial middle was a computation no human had surveyed or ever would survey. To accept it, you had to trust that the program correctly implemented the mathematics, that the compiler correctly translated the program, and that the hardware ran it faithfully for a thousand hours, and none of that trust could be discharged by reading. Philosophers argued in earnest about whether the theorem was known in the traditional sense at all. Checking the checker seemed to open an infinite regress: verify the proof with a program, and you must now verify the program, and the program that verified it, and so on.

The four-color theorem sat for a generation in this uncomfortable state: almost universally believed, and yet, by the classical standard, unsurveyed. The resolution, when it came, did not lower the standard. It relocated it.

## The small kernel

The resolution is the proof assistant, and its central design principle was articulated by the Dutch mathematician N. G. de Bruijn, whose Automath project in the late 1960s was the first serious attempt to have computers check real mathematics. De Bruijn saw the regress problem coming and answered it with an architectural rule now called the de Bruijn criterion: a proof assistant may be as large and clever as it likes, but every proof it produces must be reducible to a simple object that a very small, very simple, independent program can check on its own.

The proof assistant, in other words, is split into an untrusted part and a trusted part, and the trusted part is kept tiny. The large system helps you build proofs: it searches, automates, fills in routine steps, manages libraries. None of that machinery has to be correct, because everything it produces is handed to a small kernel that verifies each step against the primitive rules of logic, exactly the mechanical checking Hilbert imagined and Gödel formalized. Trust does not spread through the system; it concentrates in the kernel. And a kernel is a few hundred to a few thousand lines of code: short enough to be read, audited, rewritten independently, and tested against rival implementations. The infinite regress collapses into a finite and manageable duty. You need not trust the mathematician, the search machinery, or the thousand hours of computation. You need only trust one small program, and that program you can actually read.

This is not a lowering of Euclid's standard. It is Euclid's standard, mechanized. A formally verified proof is the replayable object made perfectly literal: anyone, anywhere, can rerun the check and receive the same verdict, and the fuel of unexamined intuition that Euclid's diagrams smuggled in has been removed. The axioms are declared. The rules are declared. Everything else is derivation, and the derivation is checked, in full, every time.

The approach was proved on exactly the case that caused the crisis. In 2005, Georges Gonthier and his collaborators produced a complete formal proof of the four-color theorem in the proof assistant Coq. The gigantic case analysis was not avoided; it was brought inside the logic, so that the computations themselves became steps checked by Coq's kernel. The proof that no human can survey became a proof that a small, surveyable program certifies end to end. Gonthier then led a team through a far harder target: the Feit and Thompson odd-order theorem, a landmark of group theory whose original 1963 proof filled two hundred fifty-five journal pages. The formalization took years and was completed in 2012, and along the way the team built formal libraries covering swaths of algebra. And the technology crossed from mathematics into engineering: Xavier Leroy's CompCert, a compiler for the C language, carries a machine-checked proof that it preserves the meaning of the programs it compiles. When researchers later hammered many compilers with randomized testing, they reported finding wrong-code bugs in the conventional compilers they tested, and none in CompCert's verified core. Today, proof assistants such as Coq, Isabelle, and Lean check work across mathematics and computer science, and Lean's mathematical library, built by a large open community, holds over a million lines of formalized mathematics under one kernel's discipline.

## Two integrity technologies

Now put Part I and Part II side by side, because they have been telling the same story in different costumes.

Chapter 2 described a civilization that carried its most valued texts for centuries on the most perishable medium there is: human memory. The paramparā, the unbroken teacher-to-student line, did not survive by hoping memory would hold. It engineered around memory's decay. The Vedic reciters built the permutation recitations: the pada text, which breaks the stream into isolated words; the krama text, which recites overlapping pairs; the ghana and other braided forms, which weave the words forward and backward in fixed interlocking patterns. Each mode is derived from the same base text by a fixed rule, so the modes must agree with one another everywhere. A slip that survives one mode collides with another. Error does not accumulate silently; it surfaces at the next cross-check, in the presence of a teacher whose own recitation descends through an independent line. The result, as philologists have long marveled, is textual transmission of extraordinary fidelity, sustained without writing, across a span of time that destroyed most of the world's written archives.

Chapter 5 has now described a discipline that carries its most valued arguments on carriers that also decay, through fading intuition, thinning readerships, and unsurveyable computation, and that engineered its way to the same destination: a small, independent, mechanical check that every carried object must pass, replayable by anyone, refusing everything that fails.

These are the same invention. Both are integrity technologies: systems for carrying structure across generations without decay, built by people who declined to trust memory, scribes, experts, or themselves. The parallels are close enough to tabulate.

| | Vedic paramparā | Proof assistant |
|---|---|---|
| What is carried | The exact sound-form of the text | The exact logical form of the proof |
| Carrier | Trained human memory, in lineage | Digital text, in repositories |
| Redundancy | Permutation recitations (pada, krama, ghana) derived from one base by fixed rules | The proof term, re-derivable and re-checkable from axioms by fixed rules |
| Checker | Cross-recitation agreement, teacher and co-reciter comparison | The kernel, a small program applying the primitive rules |
| Unit of trust | The discipline of the lineage, not any single reciter | The kernel, not any single prover or tactic |
| Failure response | Divergence surfaces at the next recitation and is corrected against the lineage | The build fails; the proof is rejected until repaired |
| Failure mode | Preserves sound, not sense: a perfectly carried text can be perfectly misunderstood | Preserves form, not meaning: a perfectly checked theorem can perfectly miss the point |

The last row is the most important one in this book, and it cuts both ways. The recitation technologies guarantee the syllables and guarantee nothing about interpretation; that is why the tradition grew its vast apparatus of commentary, the vṛtti and bhāṣya layers of Chapter 2, to carry the meaning that the sound-carrier could not. The kernel guarantees the theorems and guarantees nothing about whether the formalization says what you meant; that gap, between checked form and intended meaning, is precisely where Part III of this book will do its work, and where its honesty will be tested.

::: pandit
The claim here is not that proof was foreign to India and recitation was its substitute. Indian philosophy built its own rich theory of justification: the pramāṇa framework of valid means of knowing, and in Nyāya a canonical five-limb inference schema running from thesis through reason and example to conclusion, refined across centuries of formal debate. Navya-Nyāya later pushed the analysis of cognitive structure to a precision that Chapter 8 will argue maps startlingly well onto modern type theory. The parallel drawn in this chapter is deliberately narrower: the pāṭha system and the kernel solve the same transmission-integrity problem, by the same architectural instinct of cheap redundant derivations plus an independent cross-check. The inferential traditions enter this book's story two chapters from now, at the level of meaning rather than transmission.
:::

There is one more resonance worth naming before we move on. Gödel taught the formal tradition that no system certifies itself; trust in the axioms lives outside the system, in a community that examines and adopts them. The paramparā never believed otherwise. No reciter certifies himself; the lineage does, and the lineage's authority is nothing over and above the ongoing discipline of its cross-checks. Both traditions locate certainty not in any individual carrier but in an architecture of mutual verification that any qualified participant can join. That is what it means to call proof a social technology. The machine did not replace the community of checkers. It gave the community a checker whose patience is infinite and whose standards never slip.

## What the kernel cannot yet hear

So here is where the two halves of our story stand. The Sanskrit tradition built a flawless carrier for sound and a disciplined, contentious, human apparatus for meaning. The formal tradition built a flawless carrier for logical form. And the question this book exists to ask now comes into focus: can the second carrier be taught to hold the first tradition's meanings?

Not the syllables; the paramparā and the philologists have the syllables. The meanings. When a translator renders a verse of Bhartṛhari one way and a rival renders it incompatibly, each is making claims about entities, relations, and doctrines. Claims have structure. The lesson of this chapter is that once something has explicit structure, a small kernel can check it, replay it, and refuse its corruptions forever. The lesson of the next chapter is what that structure has to be. Mathematics became checkable because its statements were already formal. To make statements about meaning checkable, we need the twentieth century's deepest bridge between statements and objects: the discovery that propositions can be types, and that a proof is a program the type-checker accepts. That discovery is where the kernel gets its ears.

## The thread so far

Part I gave us a tradition that carried texts without decay by redundant recitation and lineage cross-checks, and carried meaning by commentary. This chapter followed the formal tradition's parallel search, from Euclid's replayable arguments through the collapse of intuition, Hilbert's wager, and Gödel's honest limits, to the proof assistant's answer: concentrate all trust in a small kernel that mechanically replays every step. The two traditions turn out to have built the same integrity technology for form, and to share the same open flank, meaning. Next we need the idea that lets meaning take a checkable shape: propositions as types.
