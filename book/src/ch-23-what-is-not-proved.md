# 23 · What the Theorems Do Not Prove

Begin with the ledger of what this book has actually earned, stated flat.

All 144 verse units of the Brahmakāṇḍa carry a compiled adequacy theorem: the reading this edition prints is entailed by its verse's contract and contradicts none of it. Alongside them stand 361 compiled refutations, each a named, plausible mistranslation proved to fail in a mode registered in advance: 156 assert something the commentary explicitly denies, 205 assert something no axiom licenses. With 56 auxiliary sort-error lemmas and the kernel's own involution proof, the build carries 566 theorems, every one closed by `decide`, with zero `sorry`, zero `admit`, and no proof-faking construct anywhere in the tree. A swap test ran every accepted reading against every other verse's contract and licensed zero of 20,592 ordered pairs. The whole edifice rebuilds from a clean checkout: corpus gates, contract validation, Lean compilation, the zero-sorry sweep. Every acceptance that produced it is recorded in an append-only ledger and can be replayed. The artifacts survived a structured adversarial review by six hostile reviewers told to falsify, not confirm (paper §9, §11).

That is the strongest set of claims in this book, and it is a real set. Now the sentence that gives this chapter its title.

Not one of those theorems says that the translation is faithful to Bhartṛhari.

No theorem says the contract for 1.1 captures what the Vṛtti means. No theorem says the six sorts carve the ontology where the tradition would carve it. No theorem says the accepted rendering of any verse is what a fifth-century grammarian-philosopher intended. Fidelity to Bhartṛhari is not a decidable predicate. It is not a predicate at all in any formal sense, and no amount of engineering will make it one. Chapter 9 stated this boundary in one paragraph (paper §3.4). This chapter is that paragraph given the room it deserves, because the boundary is not a disclaimer appended to the result. The boundary is the result. Knowing exactly where the machine's warrant stops is what makes the warrant worth having.

## Two links, one chain

The claim this edition makes has the shape of a chain with two links, and the two links have different natures on purpose.

Link one runs from the translation to the contract. It is machine-checked. The accepted reading, decomposed into claims, is provably covered by the contract's axioms and provably clear of its denials, and every registered rival provably fails. This link is as strong as Lean's kernel, which is to say: strong, small, independently auditable, and indifferent to everyone's opinions, including the author's.

Link two runs from the contract to the commentary. It is not machine-checked, because it cannot be. Whether the axiom `identity(śabdatattva, brahman)` is a fair formalization of the commentary's sentence about word-essence is a judgment, and judgments do not compile. What the architecture does instead is make this link auditable by construction. Every axiom, every denial, every entity carries a citation, and the citation gate guarantees that the cited sentence occurs verbatim in the commentary; every accepted-reading claim likewise cites the translation verbatim (paper §3.2). So auditing link two never requires reconstructing anyone's reasoning. It is a lookup. You place one line of JSON beside one sentence of commentary and judge whether the sentence licenses the line. That is the entire procedure.

You can run it yourself, today. Pick a verse. Open its file under `data/contracts/`. Take any axiom, read its `cite` field, and find that sentence on the verse's page in the edition. Then ask the only question that matters: does this sentence commit the commentary to this claim? If yes, move to the next axiom. If no, you have found something real, and the last section of this chapter tells you what to do with it. A single verse takes minutes. The whole book is 624 axioms and 181 denials, and a determined reader could audit every one of them in a week (paper §5). No prior edition of this text, or of any text known to the author, has made that offer, because no prior edition kept its interpretive commitments in a form where the offer means anything.

So the division of labor, stated once more and then assumed: the prover keeps commitments; the philologist judges them. Link one is a theorem. Link two is a public, itemized, checkable claim awaiting your judgment. Confusing the two links would be the one unforgivable reading of this book.

## One standpoint, inherited

Link two has a further honest qualifier, and it concerns whose commentary sits at its far end.

The translation and analytical commentary in this edition were produced against a single primary reference: K. A. Subramania Iyer's 1965 edition and annotated translation of Book I with the Vṛtti (paper §4.1). Where the Vṛtti as presented there settles a reading, this edition's commentary follows it. Where readings are disputed, the dispute is recorded in a per-verse contested note rather than silently resolved; Book I carries 56 such notes. This was a deliberate choice, and a defensible one: Iyer's Book I is the standard scholarly point of entry, and the Vṛtti is the oldest and most authoritative commentarial layer. But a choice it remains, and the contracts inherit it wholesale. Every axiom in every contract is, in the end, a formalization of one commentarial standpoint. The theorems prove the translation coherent with that standpoint. They are silent about the standpoint itself.

The tradition did not speak with one voice, and the machinery here does not pretend it did. Bhartṛhari's commentators disagree with one another, sometimes sharply, about exactly the kind of question a contract encodes: what a term denotes, which relation a verse asserts, which rival view a passage reports rather than endorses. A contract built from Vṛṣabha's ṭīkā, or from Puṇyarāja, would not be this contract. Some axioms would survive unchanged. Others would move, split, or invert.

That observation points at the most interesting extension of this whole programme, and it should be described as the opportunity it is rather than muffled as a weakness. Nothing in the architecture assumes one contract per verse. Contracts are files. A verse could carry two, keyed to two commentators, and then inter-commentarial disagreement stops being a paragraph in a monograph and becomes a diff: a machine-readable, line-by-line statement of exactly where two branches of the tradition part company, with each side's claims cited verbatim to its own commentary and each side's coherence separately proved. One could then ask compiled questions that are barely askable today. On which verses do the commentators' contracts agree entirely? Where does one commentator's axiom appear among another's denials? Does a translation exist that is adequate under both? That is future work, stated in the open, with the design already able to receive it (paper §12). It is not a defect being managed. It is the next experiment.

::: pandit
The tradition has its own name for what a contract-diff would formalize: vyākhyāna-bheda, difference of exposition. The classical apparatus for handling it is doxographic prose: a ṭīkā reports the other ācārya's view, marks it as pūrvapakṣa or as an alternative (kecit tu, "but some say"), and argues. What that apparatus never had is a way to make the disagreement exhaustive and mechanical: to guarantee that every point of divergence between two expositions is enumerated, and that nothing else diverges. Two compiled contracts for one verse would give exactly that guarantee. The scholarly labor stays where it always was, in reading the commentators well. What changes is that the comparison, once made, is total, checkable, and permanent. A reader who believes this edition has read the Vṛtti wrong has, in the contract format, a precise instrument for saying so: write the contract you believe the Vṛtti licenses, and diff.
:::

## The audit that found nothing

Chapter 11 described the pipeline's gates. One instrument in the toolchain is not a gate, and this book owes you a straight report of what it did and did not find, because the result is null and the null is instructive.

The instrument is an embedding audit (paper §10). Every verse's translation and its commentary prose are embedded in the same vector space, and verses are ranked by the cosine similarity of the pair. The intuition is plain: if a translation has drifted from its commentary, the two texts should sit far apart, so low similarity should flag trouble. A natural hypothesis follows: the 56 verses whose readings the tradition itself disputes, the contested set, should look different under this lens. Contested verses are where meaning is hardest to pin; surely they are where translation and commentary drift.

At Book-I scale, they do not. The contested flag does not separate. Contested verses average 0.567 similarity against 0.550 for uncontested verses, a difference that is small and in the unhelpful direction. The lowest tail is not enriched: of the 20 lowest-similarity verses, 7 are contested, against a 38.9 percent base rate, which is what chance would give you. Across the full 1,796-verse corpus the direction reverses and contested verses do shift left, but that observation lives outside the verified scope and earns no claims here. The honest summary is that at the scale of this book's verified object, the instrument cannot tell contested from settled (paper §10).

So the instrument is demoted, explicitly, to what its evidence supports: a review queue, not a fidelity measure. Low similarity means "an editor should look here first." It does not mean "this translation is unfaithful," and high similarity certainly does not mean "this translation is faithful." Used as a queue, it earns its keep; its head is informative in practice.

Why report a null result at all, in a trade book, at this length? Because publishing the null is part of the discipline the whole project claims to practice. A programme that reports its gates catching fabricated citations, and its red team landing a real philological finding, forfeits its credibility the day it quietly shelves an instrument that failed to perform. The audit was built, run, and reported, and what it showed is that a plausible fidelity proxy does not discriminate at this scale. That is a finding. It now stands in the paper and in this chapter so that no reader mistakes a cosine similarity for a theorem, least of all the author.

::: vrtti
The queue's head shows why the instrument survives in demoted form. The lowest-ranked verse in Book I is 1.74, at similarity 0.301, and it is a contested verse. Its commentary is dominated by a doxographic dispute, rival positions on whether the sentence-sphoṭa doctrine belongs to the siddhānta, that the brief translation cannot and should not carry. Translation and commentary sit far apart in the vector space because they are doing different jobs, not because the translation is wrong. The instrument flagged exactly the right verse for editorial attention and was exactly wrong about why. That is what a review queue is: a pointer, whose every deliverance a human must still read.
:::

## What the formalism cannot carry

The boundary has an outer face, fidelity, and an inner face: what the formal language itself was built unable to say. Three deliberate poverties, owned in turn (paper §12).

First, entailment is shallow. The kernel's entailment relation is little more than closure of the literal claims under the symmetry of identity. Adequacy is therefore membership-like, not inferential: an accepted claim must in effect be present among the axioms, up to symmetric identity, rather than derivable from them by a chain of reasoning. The kernel cannot chain relations, cannot compose an identity with a predication across steps it was not handed, cannot draw a consequence. This was a choice, and Chapter 7 showed what it buys: every verdict in the book is a terminating computation, which is why `decide` closes 566 theorems and why the Python mirror can predict the kernel exactly. A richer relation, transitive sambandha chains, defeasible generics, would make the theorems say more and would put decidability, and with it the entire everything-by-computation discipline, at risk. The trade was taken with eyes open, and the cost is stated here: these theorems check coverage, not consequence.

Second, six sorts are coarse. Absolute, power, manifestation, linguisticItem, property, cognition: the menu is small enough to hold in the head, and Chapter 12 showed it doing real work, forcing 41 terms into 91 justified senses. But Bhartṛhari's world is not six-sorted. Persons are the clearest casualty: the child of 1.121 and 1.151, who figures in the text as a knower whose first cognitions the argument needs, is shoehorned into the frame as an epistemic subject because nothing better exists on the menu. Every ontology clips; this one clips visibly, and the registry records where the clipping forced a ruling.

Third, and most important: a contract formalizes the formalizable core of a verse, not the verse. The lamp and its light, the fire resident in the churning-sticks, the yolk of the peahen's egg: Book I argues through images, ordering, resonance, and deliberate ambiguity, and almost none of that survives into entities and claims, nor should it. The contract for 1.1 is four axioms and one denial. The verse is thirty-two syllables that a tradition has found inexhaustible. When a chapter of this book says a verse's meaning is "verified," the word reaches exactly as far as the contract's claims and no further. The remainder, which is most of what makes the text worth reading, is carried where it always was: in the translation's prose, the commentary, and the reader.

::: engineer
The sharpest version of the inner-face objection came from the adversarial review, and it deserves its exact statement. Since author and contract-writer coincide, each accepted reading is by construction a subset of axioms the same process wrote; 48 of the 144 are exact matches. So `accepted_adequate` verifies the internal coherence of the author's choices, not their independent correctness. Correct, and it is precisely the §3.4 boundary restated. The objection has bite only if the contracts lack discriminative content, if they are so loose that anything passes. That version is falsifiable, and it was tested: run every verse's accepted reading against every other verse's contract, 144 by 143, and count what is licensed. The answer is zero of 20,592, and the swap test is now a permanent regression gate. Each contract licenses its own reading and no other's. Coherence, yes, only coherence; but coherence against a reference specific enough to reject 20,592 near-neighbors is not vacuous, and any edit loose enough to make two verses interchangeable now breaks the build (paper §11).
:::

## The strongest criticism is a pull request

Everything in this chapter converges on an unusual property: this edition is built to be attacked, and it specifies its own attack surface.

The contracts are public. They are plain JSON in a public repository, one file per verse, alongside the kernel, the generated proofs, the corpus with its checksum, and the ledger of every acceptance. They are diffable: an interpretive disagreement with this edition can be expressed as a one-line change to a file, and the meaning of that change is not left to rhetoric. Change the file, run the build, and the machine reports the consequences by name: these theorems now fail, this rival reading is now licensed, this denial now collides with that axiom. Disagreement becomes checkable, in both directions. If your change breaks the build, the build names exactly which of the edition's commitments you have contradicted, which is to say, it names the real point at issue. If your change passes, you have proved something too: that the tradition's text, as this commentary transmits it, underdetermines the choice between your reading and this edition's, at the resolution the contracts can see. Either outcome is knowledge. Either outcome took minutes.

This is not a hypothetical courtesy. The process has already run at full hostility once, and the edition is better for it. The adversarial review's one confirmed major finding was philological: nine doxographic verses had encoded views the text merely reports as if the text endorsed them, exactly the mistake a verbatim-citation gate cannot catch, since the reporting sentences really do occur. The fix, the reported stance that separates what the text asserts from what it surveys, now covers 32 axioms across those nine verses, and the finding, the verification, and the repair are all in the ledger (paper §11). A stranger read the artifacts with intent to break them, found a real flaw, and the flaw became structure. That is the invitation, demonstrated. The most valuable thing a reader of this book can do with it is not to admire the theorem count. It is to find the axiom that is wrong.

So the challenge, plainly. If you believe this edition misreads a verse, do not write that it "fails to capture the richness" of the original; that sentence has been written about every translation of everything and has never once been checkable. Open the contract. Find the axiom you dispute, or the axiom you find missing, or the citation you judge misapplied. Change it. Watch the build. Then send the change, with the build's verdict attached, and the disagreement will exist in a form that can be adjudicated on textual merits and, once adjudicated, protected by regression forever. Criticism of this edition has a compiler now. Use it.

## What a type-checker settles

A tradition that has argued about these verses for two millennia will not be settled by a type-checker. It should not be. The arguments are the tradition; the Vṛtti disputes readings, the ṭīkās dispute the Vṛtti, and this book's contested notes are one more layer of the same honest quarrel. Nothing in this project closes that conversation, and a project that claimed to would have misunderstood both the text and the proofs.

What changed is smaller and harder than settlement. One edition of Book I now states its interpretive commitments explicitly: 776 entities, 624 axioms, 181 denials, every one cited to a sentence, every consequence compiled. Its coherence is a theorem. Its fidelity is a claim, itemized and public, that any reader can now audit by lookup and contest by diff. For two thousand years, disagreeing with a translation meant writing another one. As of this edition, disagreeing can mean pointing, precisely, at the exact commitment where the paths divide, and letting a machine hold both sides to what they said. The theorems do not prove the translation true. They make it the first translation of this text that can be attacked with precision. That was the point.

## The thread so far

The vine walked, the claims are weighed. Machine-checked: 144 adequacy theorems and 361 refutations, zero sorry, replayable from a ledger, zero cross-licensing among 20,592 swapped pairs. Not machine-checked, and never to be: fidelity to Bhartṛhari, which the architecture instead makes auditable by lookup, since every commitment cites its licensing sentence verbatim; that audit inherits the standpoint of one primary source, Iyer 1965 with the Vṛtti, and rival commentaries would yield rival contracts, a future diff rather than a hidden flaw. The embedding audit reported its null straight: at Book-I scale the contested flag does not separate, so the instrument is a review queue, nothing more. Entailment is shallow, the sorts are six and coarse, the contracts hold a verse's formalizable core only, and the strongest criticism the edition can receive is a pull request that changes a contract and lets the build speak.

*Doors: engineers, the boundary discipline is the export: verify link one, instrument link two, publish the nulls, and make your critics' strongest move a diff. Proof people, the engineer box holds the tautology objection and its mechanical answer; note what shallow entailment buys and what it costs. Commentary scholars, this chapter was written for you: the standpoint is named, the audit procedure is a lookup, and the pandit box sketches how vyākhyāna-bheda becomes data. Literature readers, you now know exactly what "verified" does and does not mean on every page you have read. Vyākaraṇa students, the contested notes and the reported stance are the tradition's own doxographic manners, kept; Chapter 24 asks what the prover would need to learn your grammar properly.*
