# A Few Notes {.unnumbered}

## On the Sanskrit {.unnumbered}

Sanskrit appears in this book in two forms, always together. Devanagari,
the script in which the verses are traditionally printed, carries the
mūla (root) text of every verse. Transliteration in the IAST scheme (the
international standard, with marks like ā, ṛ, ś, and ṇ) accompanies
every Devanagari passage, so nothing depends on your device rendering
the script. In running prose, Sanskrit terms appear in IAST alone.

You do not need to read Devanagari to read this book. If you would like
to, or want to pronounce what you read, Appendix B gives the full letter
table and a plain guide to the sounds. Two habits repay the effort
immediately: long vowels (ā, ī, ū) take twice the time of short ones,
and the retroflex consonants (ṭ, ḍ, ṇ, ṣ) curl the tongue back.

Verse references use the traditional numbering: 1.42 is the forty-second
kārikā of Book One. Some units carry ranges (1.18-22) where the
commentary treats a run of verses together; this book follows the
commentary's own units, all 144 of them. The title of the treatise is
given throughout in its Sanskrit form, Vākyapadīyam; scholarly
literature in English often cites it as Vākyapadīya, and both name the
same text.

## On how this edition was made {.unnumbered}

Full candor, before anything else is said. The English translation and
the analytical commentary that this book presents and verifies were
produced by a machine-assisted process working from the Sanskrit, with
K. A. Subramania Iyer's edition and annotated translation of Book One
with the Vṛtti (1965) as the primary reference text. The formal
contracts that hold the translation to the commentary were likewise
drafted by machine, under mechanical gates described in Part III. What
the theorems certify is the coherence of this whole structure: that the
accepted translation of each verse satisfies a contract whose every
axiom quotes this edition's commentary verbatim, and that hundreds of
rival renderings provably fail. Whether the commentary itself is
faithful to Bhartṛhari remains, as it always was, a question for human
judgment; the book keeps that judgment cheap by citing, everywhere, the
exact sentence on which each decision rests. Chapter 1 tells this story
properly, and Chapter 23 examines its limits without flinching.

## On the verification claims {.unnumbered}

Every number in this book (144 verses, 361 refuted misreadings, zero
unfinished proofs) is computed from a public repository that rebuilds
from source, and Appendix D indexes every theorem by verse. Where this
book says proved, it means a specific machine-checked theorem exists
and can be replayed on your own computer with the commands in
Appendix A.
