# A Note on Sanskrit

Sanskrit appears in this book in two forms, always together. Devanagari,
the script in which the verses are traditionally printed, is used for the
mūla (root) text of every verse. Transliteration in the IAST scheme (the
international standard, with marks like ā, ṛ, ś, and ṇ) accompanies every
Devanagari passage, so nothing depends on your device rendering the
script. In running prose, Sanskrit terms appear in IAST alone.

You do not need to read Devanagari to read this book. If you would like
to, or if you want to pronounce what you read, Appendix B gives the full
letter table and a plain guide to the sounds. Two habits repay the
effort immediately: long vowels (ā, ī, ū) take twice the time of short
ones, and the retroflex consonants (ṭ, ḍ, ṇ, ṣ) curl the tongue back.

Verse references use the traditional numbering: 1.42 is the forty-second
kārikā of Book One. Some units carry ranges (1.18-22) where the
commentary treats a run of verses together; this book follows the
commentary's own units, all 144 of them.
