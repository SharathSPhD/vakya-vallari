# 20 · Cognition Interwoven with the Word

*Verses 1.123 to 1.131 · 9 units*

The report of the last chapter ended by turning inward. This movement, 1.123 to 1.131, makes the turn its whole subject. Its thesis is stated in the first verse, and it is the single claim for which Bhartṛhari is remembered in the history of philosophy: there is no cognition anywhere that does not have the word woven into it. Not thought that can afterward be put into words, but thought that is already worded in the having of it. The nine verses take that thesis, defend it against its sharpest opponent, widen it to the outer edge of sentience, and then turn it into a path.

The sequence is tight. 1.123 states the thesis. 1.124 proves it, and proves it transcendentally: if word-formedness were to depart from awareness, the light of consciousness would not shine, because it is that word-formedness which makes recognition possible at all. From necessity the text moves to scope. 1.125 widens the claim horizontally, making word the binder of every science, craft, and art. 1.126 widens it to the limit, making the word-power coextensive with sentience itself, in all the kinds of beings and not humans alone. 1.127 draws out a compact metaphysics of action: in the state of division the word-power mediates between agent and deed, and in non-division it is the deed. 1.128 brings the claim down into semantic practice, where objects stabilize as they are said, and 1.129 presses it to the limiting case, the whirling fire-brand circle, where language supplies a form that reality does not. Then the soteriology begins. 1.130 adduces, by citation, the inner word as the great bull with which union is sought, and 1.131 closes on tasmāt: the perfecting of the word is the attainment of the supreme self, and one who knows the principle of its functioning enjoys the deathless Brahman.

The thesis has a named adversary, and the chapter is shaped by it. Against Bhartṛhari stands the Buddhist doctrine of nirvikalpaka pratyakṣa, bare wordless perception, a cognition that grasps its object before any concept or name arrives. 1.123 denies exactly that. The contract records the denial as a formal fact, and every rejected reading that smuggles a wordless stratum back in, whether as raw perception or as thought first and label second, runs straight into it. Readers who arrive expecting the familiar picture, in which experience comes first and words are fitted to it afterward, will find that picture named and refused on the first card.

Three cards reward a slow read, and one deserves a second visit. Slow down at 1.124, because it is the whole argument in one verse: it turns the thesis from an assertion into a necessity by showing that awareness cannot recognize anything without the word-formedness it would have to lack. Slow down at 1.126, because it is the widest claim in the sequence, and because a single word, samjñā, is doing double duty beneath the translation, meaning both consciousness and naming, so that a being's very awareness is read as its naming. Slow down at 1.129, because the fire-brand circle is the cleanest demonstration in the book: there is no circle in the fire, only a moving point, and the circle the mind sees is supplied by the word. And return to 1.127, because the shift it teaches, from word-as-instrument to word-as-deed, is the hinge on which the soteriology of 1.130 and 1.131 turns.

In the cards, one verdict dominates: contradicted. The commentary here does not merely omit the opposing view, it denies it, so readings that reinstate a wordless perception (1.123) or that make objects wear determinate contours before speech reports them (1.128) fail as contradicted rather than as unlicensed. The other pole is demotion, the failure mode the walk has met before. Watch it recur where a reading shrinks vāk from an absolute principle to a passive label: word as a mere descriptive tool at 1.123, word as a device that only names sciences at 1.125, śabda-saṃskāra reduced to mechanical grammatical skill at 1.131. Each of those demotes something the commentary treats as constitutive to a mere property, and each fails as unlicensed, a sort error. Watch too for the subtler miss at 1.127, where a reading keeps the mediation relation switched on in both states and so contradicts the very shift the verse exists to teach.

The contested boxes are fewer here than in the phoneticians' report, but two are worth pausing on. The note at 1.124 marks the real stakes of the whole chapter: how intellectualist one makes pratyavamarśinī decides how strong a thesis the verse proves. The weakest reading, that all judgement is verbal, is nearly trivial; the strongest, that all sentience is verbal, is the thesis the later Śaiva tradition embraced, and the Vṛtti pushes toward the strong reading, extending the word-association into sleep and into the first indeterminate cognitions so that no wordless stratum of awareness is left at all. The note at 1.126 records the samjñā pun and admits that either English word loses half of it. These are not hedges. They mark exactly where the kārikā underdetermines its own reach.

By its last verse this chapter has carried the argument from a claim about perception to a promise about liberation. The word is in the seeing, the seeing is in all beings, and the perfecting of the word is the attainment of the self. What it has not yet asked is how any of this reaches a learner. That question, how word-knowledge is transmitted and why it cannot be dispensed with, is the work of the next chapter, which turns from cognition to tradition.

## Featured verse: 1.123 in slow motion

No cognition without the word interwoven in it: the section's thesis verse. The contract renders the interweaving as a relation on the cognition sort, and the rejected reading, which makes language a dispensable label on thought, asserts exactly what the denial forbids.

**Axioms, each with its verbatim license.**

- relation `anugamadapeksam(pratyaya → sabda)` — “no cognition whatsoever without the accompaniment of word”
- predication `anuviddha(jnana)` — “as if interwoven, shot through, with word”
- relation `vivartate(sabda → jnana)` — “Cognition is not first accomplished wordlessly and then labelled; the wording is *in* the seeing, as the thread is in the fabric — remove it and no fabric remains”
- predication `arthakriyakari(sabda)` — “attend to any actual cognition and the attending is already articulate”

**Denials.**

- `anugamadapeksam(nirvikalpaka_pratyaksa → sabda)` — “Against this stands the great counter-tradition — the Buddhist doctrine that perception proper (nirvikalpaka pratyaksa) is precisely wordless”
- `utpatti_anantaram_labha(jnana → sabda)` — “Cognition is not first accomplished wordlessly and then labelled”

**Rejected readings and their compiled fates.**

- “Perception can occur in a wordless state, with conceptualization and verbal articulation added afterwards.” → **contradicted**. The commentary explicitly rejects the Buddhist doctrine of nirvikalpaka pratyaksa (wordless perception), stating that attending to actual cognition reveals it is already articulate. This reading allows a mode of cognition without word dependency.
- “Knowledge is first independently accomplished, and then subsequently labeled with words.” → **contradicted**. Commits the dualism the verse and commentary reject: cognition first, word second. The commentary states 'the wording is *in* the seeing, as the thread is in the fabric'; remove the thread and fabric remains is false. Word is constitutive, not post-hoc attribution.
- “Word is merely a tool for describing cognitions that can occur and be known independently of it.” → **unlicensed**. Demotes word to a property (descriptive instrument) rather than recognizing it as constitutive to the structure of all knowing. The sort error (property vs. linguisticItem with constitutive role) treats the relation as contingent rather than necessary.

**Theorems in `V1_123.lean`.** `accepted_adequate`, `wordless_perception_inadequate`, `post_hoc_labeling_inadequate`, `linguistic_description_only_inadequate`, `linguistic_description_only_sort_error`. All close by `decide`; the build carries zero `sorry`.


## The verses

### 1.123

::: {.deva}
न सोऽस्ति प्रत्ययो लोके यः शब्दानुगमादृते ।  
अनुविद्धमिव ज्ञानं सर्वं शब्देन भासते ॥ १२३ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
na so'sti pratyayo loke yaḥ śabdānugamādṛte |  
anuviddhamiva jñānaṃ sarvaṃ śabdena bhāsate || 123 ||
:::

> There is no cognition in the world that does not follow upon word; all knowledge appears as if interpenetrated by word.

*Gist.* The most celebrated karika of the work, and the thesis for which Bhartrhari stands in the history of philosophy.

*Contract.* 5 entities · 4 axioms · 2 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_123`.

*Refuted.* “Perception can occur in a wordless state, with conceptualization and verbal articulation added afterwards.” (contradicted): The commentary explicitly rejects the Buddhist doctrine of nirvikalpaka pratyaksa (wordless perception), stating that attending to actual cognition reveals it is already articulate.


### 1.124

::: {.deva}
वाग्रूपता चेदुत्क्रामेदवबोधस्य शाश्वती ।  
न प्रकाशः प्रकाशेत सा हि प्रत्यवमर्शिनी ॥ १२४ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
vāgrūpatā cedutkrāmedavabodhasya śāśvatī |  
na prakāśaḥ prakāśeta sā hi pratyavamarśinī || 124 ||
:::

> If that eternal word-formedness were to depart from awareness, the light would not shine — for it is that which makes recognition possible.

*Gist.* The transcendental argument for 1.123 — from assertion to necessity in one verse.

*Contract.* 4 entities · 4 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_124`.

*Refuted.* “The light of consciousness shines independently, even without word-formedness or articulation.” (contradicted): The verse explicitly denies this: prakasha cannot illuminate if vagrupa departs from avabodha.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* pratyavamarsini we render 'that which makes recognition possible', taking pratyavamarsa as the determining 'taking-as' within awareness; the term admits shades from 'reflective judgement' to 'introspection', and how intellectualist one makes it decides how strong a thesis the verse proves — the weakest reading (all *judgement* is verbal) is nearly trivial, the strongest (all *sentience* is verbal) is the one the Saiva tradition embraced. The Vrtti supports the strong reading: it extends the word-association into sleep and into indeterminate first-cognitions (which still grasp their objects 'as this or that'), leaving no wordless stratum of awareness at all
:::


### 1.125

::: {.deva}
सा सर्वविद्याशिल्पानां कलानां चोपबन्धनी ।  
तद्वशादभिनिष्पन्नं सर्वं वस्तु विभज्यते ॥ १२५ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
sā sarvavidyāśilpānāṃ kalānāṃ copabandhanī |  
tadvaśādabhiniṣpannaṃ sarvaṃ vastu vibhajyate || 125 ||
:::

> It is the binder of all sciences, crafts and arts; by its power everything that is brought forth is differentiated.

*Gist.* The scope of pratyavamarsini vak, spelled out horizontally.

*Contract.* 6 entities · 5 axioms · 0 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_125`.

*Refuted.* “Sciences, crafts and arts have their own structure independent of word; the word merely labels or describes them.” (unlicensed): Demotes vāk from the absolute binding principle to a passive property or linguistic labeling device, contradicting the commentary's insistence that word-power actively binds (upabandhati) all domains…


### 1.126

::: {.deva}
सैषा संसारिणां संज्ञा बहिरन्तश्च वर्तते ।  
तन्मात्रामव्यतिक्रान्तं चैतन्यं सर्वजातिषु ॥ १२६ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
saiṣā saṃsāriṇāṃ saṃjñā bahirantaśca vartate |  
tanmātrāmavyatikrāntaṃ caitanyaṃ sarvajātiṣu || 126 ||
:::

> This it is that operates as the consciousness of transmigrating beings, without and within; sentience in all the kinds of beings does not step beyond its measure.

*Gist.* The widest claim of the sequence: the word-power is coextensive with sentience itself.

*Contract.* 8 entities · 5 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_126`.

*Refuted.* “This consciousness operates only in human transmigrating beings, in speech and thought.” (unlicensed): Restricts caitanya to humans alone, ignoring the explicit universalization sarvajatisu ('in all kinds of beings').

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* samjna we render as 'consciousness' (its upanisadic sense), though 'name/designation' — its grammatical sense used throughout 1.59-69 — is deliberately audible: beings' very awareness is their 'naming'; the pun resists translation and either English choice loses half
:::


### 1.127

::: {.deva}
प्रविभागे यथा कर्ता तया कार्ये प्रवर्तते ।  
अविभागे तथा सैव कार्यत्वेनावतिष्ठते ॥ १२७ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
pravibhāge yathā kartā tayā kārye pravartate |  
avibhāge tathā saiva kāryatvenāvatiṣṭhate || 127 ||
:::

> As, in the state of differentiation, the agent proceeds by means of it toward the deed, so, in non-differentiation, it is that very power which stands as the deed itself.

*Gist.* A compact metaphysics of action, closing the loop between word and work.

*Contract.* 3 entities · 3 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_127`.

*Refuted.* “Word-power serves as a mediating instrument between agent and deed in both the divided and undivided states.” (contradicted): Asserts that the sadhana (mediation) relation persists universally, but the verse teaches a shift: in differentiation it mediates, in non-differentiation it IS the deed (no mediation).


### 1.128

::: {.deva}
स्वमात्रा परमात्रा वा श्रुत्या प्रक्रम्यते यथा ।  
तथैव रूढतामेति तया ह्यर्थो विधीयते ॥ १२८ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
svamātrā paramātrā vā śrutyā prakramyate yathā |  
tathaiva rūḍhatāmeti tayā hyartho vidhīyate || 128 ||
:::

> Whether as its own measure or as the measure of another — however it is set going by the utterance, just so does it become established; for it is by utterance that the object is determined.

*Gist.* How word-dependence cashes out in semantic practice: things stabilize as they are said.

*Contract.* 7 entities · 4 axioms · 2 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_128`.

*Refuted.* “Objects possess determinate contours before being reported by speech, which merely describes existing objective realities.” (contradicted): Directly contradicts the commentary's explicit statement that objects do not pre-exist with determinate form; speech does not report pre-existing contours but creates them through utterance.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* sva-matra para-matra is elliptical and our construal (own measure/another's measure = literal/transferred presentation) is one of several; the pair has also been taken as 'in its own form or that of another word' (phonological adaptation) — the verse's point, that establishment follows utterance, survives any of these
:::


### 1.129

::: {.deva}
अत्यन्तमतथाभूते निमित्ते श्रुत्युपाश्रयात् ।  
दृश्यतेऽलातचक्रादौ वस्त्वाकार निरूपणा ॥ १२९ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
atyantamatathābhūte nimitte śrutyupāśrayāt |  
dṛśyate'lātacakrādau vastvākāra nirūpaṇā || 129 ||
:::

> Even where the occasioning ground is utterly not so, by resort to the utterance one sees the determination of a thing-form — as in the fire-brand circle and the like.

*Gist.* The limiting case that proves 1.128: language determining an object where reality supplies none.

*Contract.* 5 entities · 3 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_129`.

*Refuted.* “The whirling torch through perceptual contact naturally produces the circular form.” (contradicted): Attributes form-production to perception (the alata/torch), contradicting the verse's explicit point that it is the word which supplies the cakra.


### 1.130

::: {.deva}
अपि प्रयोक्तुरात्मानं शब्दमन्तरवस्थितम् ।  
प्राहुर्महान्तमृषभं येन सायुज्यमिष्यते ॥ १३० ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
api prayokturātmānaṃ śabdamantaravasthitam |  
prāhurmahāntamṛṣabhaṃ yena sāyujyamiṣyate || 130 ||
:::

> Moreover, they declare the word standing within the speaker's self to be the great bull — union with which is what is sought.

*Gist.* The kanda's soteriology begins, and it begins with a citation.

*Contract.* 7 entities · 5 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_130`.

*Refuted.* “The audible fourth part of speech standing within the speaker is declared to be the great bull.” (contradicted): The commentary explicitly contrasts the inner word (sabdantar) with the audible fourth part: 'not the audible fourth part but the inner totality of speech'.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* the rsabha allusion is certain in tenor but its exact source and the compound maha-antam-rsabham admit variant resolutions; we follow the standard identification with the Vedic bull-of-speech imagery, and note that the karika's api prahuh marks it explicitly as received teaching being adduced, not argued
:::


### 1.131

::: {.deva}
तस्माद्यः शब्दसंस्कारः सा सिद्धिः परमात्मनः ।  
तस्य प्रवृत्तितत्त्वज्ञस्तद्ब्रह्मामृतमश्नुते ॥ १३१ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
tasmādyaḥ śabdasaṃskāraḥ sā siddhiḥ paramātmanaḥ |  
tasya pravṛttitattvajñastadbrahmāmṛtamaśnute || 131 ||
:::

> Therefore that perfecting of the word is the attainment of the supreme self; one who knows the principle of its functioning enjoys the deathless Brahman.

*Gist.* The conclusion of the whole kanda's argument, marked tasmat.

*Contract.* 6 entities · 4 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_131`.

*Refuted.* “Those who master the technical rules of grammar automatically attain the supreme self.” (unlicensed): Reduces sabda-samskara to mechanical linguistic skill rather than the deeper principle (pravrtti-tattva) of the word's functioning.
