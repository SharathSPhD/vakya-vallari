# 22 · The Levels of Speech

*Verses 1.142 to 1.155 · 14 units*

The last movement of the book climbs. It names the levels of speech, and then, past the highest of them, it points to a station higher still, and in doing so it closes the Brahmakāṇḍa. The three levels are vaikharī, the elaborated, which is audible, articulated, and ridden with sequence; madhyamā, the middle, which is the inner word, sequenced in thought but never sounded; and paśyantī, the seeing, which is undivided, with its sequence swallowed, intention without articulation. Beyond even that triad, 1.142 names a supreme station, param padam, and the apparatus identifies it with the śabda-tattva of the very first verse. The book that opened with the word-Brahman closes by making the ascent to it a climb through one's own speaking, from voice to mind to vision.

![The three levels of speech as nested arcs, from inner vision outward to uttered sound and back again.](book/assets/figures/fig13-levels-of-speech.png "Diagram of three concentric arcs, paśyantī the unitary vision innermost, madhyamā the inner articulation in the middle, and vaikharī the uttered sound outermost, with an outward arrow labeled manifestation and an inward arrow labeled return.")

The chapter is not only the ladder. After 1.142 names the triad and 1.143 fixes the summit as invariant through its own descents, neither divided by division nor increased by return, the text circles back to the two canons and gives them their formal statuses. 1.144 declares śruti beginningless, unbroken, and authorless, while smṛti, though composed by the cultivated, is yet never discontinued. 1.145 offers a daring epistemology of revelation, in which the naming in śruti is likened to the naming in a dream. 1.146 maps the three disciplines to the three seats of fault: medicine for the body, grammar for speech, metaphysics for the mind. Then comes a long closing meditation on the corrupt word, apabhraṃśa, running from 1.147 to the last verse. It defines corruption as intention-relative (1.147), makes correctness itself relative to domain (1.148), and works out how a corrupt form still manages to mean, by inference and through the correct form it stands in for, with the child's indistinct amba, amba as its model (1.149 to 1.152). Then it drives the theory into its hardest case, where corrupt forms have become a whole community's norm and the correct form is, for those speakers, the one that does not express (1.153). Finally it sets the two ideologies of language change face to face (1.154) and closes on a single kārikā (1.155).

Four cards reward a slow read. Slow down at 1.144, because it completes the registry work of the previous chapter, giving śruti and smṛti two eternalities of different types: śruti eternal in substance, or as the recurrent seed, smṛti eternal only as an unbroken continuity of composition. Slow down at 1.145, because svapnavat, dream-like, is the crux of the whole verse: it characterizes the mode of śruti's showing, its immediacy and unconstructedness, and not the truth of what is shown. Slow down at 1.154, because it is where the author sets his own view, that divine speech has been overspread by incapable speakers, beside the opposite view of those who see language as non-eternal, and then declines to refute them. And slow down at 1.155, the final verse of the kāṇḍa in this recension, which generalizes the intention-relativity of 1.147 into a principle wide enough to reach any word employed for the sake of another.

In the cards, watch the registry once more. At 1.144 a reading that makes smṛti eternal in the same sort as śruti fails as unlicensed, because the two eternalities are of different kinds and the verse is emphatic about it. Elsewhere the verdicts fall into a familiar rhythm. At 1.143 a reading that makes the summit really change substance fails as contradicted. At 1.145 a reading that presses the dream simile toward illusion, making śruti's teaching unreal, fails as contradicted. At 1.146 a reading that assigns medicine to the faults of speech fails as unlicensed, a simple domain mismatch. Across the apabhraṃśa cluster, every reading that grants the corrupt form an independent power to express fails, most as contradicted (1.150, 1.152, 1.153, 1.155) and one as unlicensed (1.147). And watch for a rarer failure mode at 1.154, where a reading projects a refutation onto the text that the text does not make, since the commentary is explicit that Bhartṛhari leaves the opposing view standing. Over-reading the author's stance is itself a way to fail the gate.

The contested notes gather at two places. The first is 1.142 itself, which carries the classical crux of the verse: whether it teaches three levels crowned by a supreme station, or already implies the four-level scheme, with para vāc, that the commentators and the Śaiva tradition later made canonical. The Vṛtti reads toward the higher systematization; the kārikā's own syntax, the supreme station of the threefold speech, supports the minimal reading, and the edition translates minimally. The second is the apabhraṃśa cluster, which is contested less on syntax than on doctrine: is the drift of speech into corruption a regrettable pathology or simply how language communities work? 1.153 and 1.154 show the school itself dividing on exactly that question. The note at 1.155 adds one last honesty: the kārikā-count of Book I varies between recensions, this text ending at 155 where other editions carry further closing verses.

With that, the vine is complete. The Brahmakāṇḍa has been walked end to end, 144 units in all, and every one of them stands as a card carrying at least one refuted reading with its compiled verdict. The distinctions the vine set out to hold, apparent division from real division, means from likeness, report from doctrine, seed from recited text, corruption from correct form, are all still standing, and they are standing because the machine could not make them fall: the builds close by decide, and they carry zero sorry. The book began at 1.1 by declaring the word to be Brahman, and it ends at 1.155 with the word's summit named and the humblest corrupt syllable given its exact place. Part V now steps back from the verses to ask the questions the theorems cannot answer for themselves: what they do not prove, what a prover tuned to Sanskrit would still need, and what all of this has been an invitation toward.

## Featured verse: 1.142 in slow motion

The levels-of-speech sequence closes the kāṇḍa. The featured contract stratifies vaikharī, madhyamā, and paśyantī as distinct entities under one power, and the rejected reading that collapses them into registers of volume fails unlicensed. The vine ends where it began: distinctions held, by theorem.

**Rejected readings and their compiled fates.**

- “Four entirely distinct and non-hierarchical levels of speech exist: vaikharī, madhyamā, paśyantī, and a separate para-vac level.” → **unlicensed**. The verse speaks of the 'param padam of the threefold vac', suggesting a summit or culmination of the three-level hierarchy rather than an additional level standing apart from them. The commentary leaves this ambiguous on purpose, whether param padam is paśyantī's absolute aspect or the later-systematized para-vac, but the karika's syntax supports the minimal reading of param padam as the pinnacle of the existing three.
- “Vaikharī, madhyamā, and paśyantī are three unrelated modes of speech with no connection between them.” → **unlicensed**. The commentary establishes each as a stage in a unified gradient: 'the śabda-tattva of 1.1 stands at the summit of an accessible gradient, which makes the return-path of 1.131 (union by the word's perfecting) a climb through one's own speaking, from voice through mind to vision.' This explicit description of ascent through the levels contradicts a reading that treats them as disconnected.
- “The three levels of speech, vaikharī, madhyamā, and paśyantī, constitute the final, ultimate reality; there is no station beyond them.” → **contradicted**. The verse's central assertion, marked as 'adbhuta' (wonder), is precisely that there IS a supreme station (param padam) beyond the threefold speech. To deny the existence of param padam directly contradicts the verse's primary claim.

## The verses

### 1.142

::: {.deva}
वैखर्या मध्यमायाश्च पश्यन्त्याश्चैतदद्भुतम् ।  
अनेकतीर्थभेदायास्त्रय्या वाचः परं पदम् ॥ १४२ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
vaikharyā madhyamāyāśca paśyantyāścaitadadbhutam |  
anekatīrthabhedāyāstrayyā vācaḥ paraṃ padam || 142 ||
:::

> This is the wonder: that beyond the threefold speech, vaikharī, madhyamā and paśyantī, with their many distinct fords, there is the supreme station of the Word.

The kanda's celebrated hierarchy, named at last. The stages have been operating incognito since 1.112-117 (utterance; the word in mind and breath; the knower in the subtle speech-self); now the names: vaikharī, the elaborated, audible, articulated, sequence-ridden speech; madhyamā, the middle, the inner word, mentally deployed, sequenced in thought though unsounded; paśyantī, the seeing, speech at the station where it *sees* rather than says: undivided, sequence-swallowed, intentionality without articulation. Aneka-tirtha-bheda: each level has many fords, many descents and crossings, dialects and deliveries at the gross end, modes of inner discourse and vision at the subtle. And the adbhuta, the declared wonder: trayyah vacah param padam, beyond even the triad, a supreme station of speech. The wording is a deliberate re-sounding of the oldest text in this lineage: the Rgveda's four quarters of vac, three hidden and the fourth what men speak (RV 1.164.45), and the padam of catvari padani. Whether that fourth is para vac as a distinct level (as the later tradition, especially Kashmir Saiva, systematized it, para as a fourth above paśyantī) or paśyantī itself in its absolute aspect is left genuinely open by the karika. The doctrinal work is done either way: speech is depth-structured, its audible face is its furthest emanation, and the śabda-tattva of 1.1 stands at (or as) the summit of an accessible gradient, which makes the return-path of 1.131 (union by the word's perfecting) a climb through one's own speaking, from voice through mind to vision.

### 1.143

::: {.deva}
तद्विभागाविभागाभ्यां क्रियमाणमवस्थितम् ।  
स्वभावज्ञैश्च भावानां दृश्यन्ते शब्दशक्तयः ॥ १४३ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
tadvibhāgāvibhāgābhyāṃ kriyamāṇamavasthitam |  
svabhāvajñaiśca bhāvānāṃ dṛśyante śabdaśaktayaḥ || 143 ||
:::

> That, whether undergoing division or non-division, remains established; and the powers of words are seen by those who know the nature of things.

The invariance of the summit through its own descents. Tat, the supreme word-station of 1.142, kriyamanam vibhaga-avibhagabhyam, being done into division and non-division, worked through both conditions (uttered into vaikharī's differentiation, gathered into paśyantī's unity), avasthitam: stands, unaltered. The doctrine is 1.75-77 and 1.101 lifted to the top of the hierarchy, as the sphoṭa is undivided through fast and slow delivery, the param padam is unmoved through the entire cycle of speech's emanation and return; levels are its vṛtti-bheda, never its own change. Second line: śabda-saktayah, the powers of words, the whole system of them, from mantra-efficacy (1.138) to world-binding (1.118), drsyante svabhava-jnaih bhavanam: are *seen* by knowers of the natures of things. Drsyante is the verb of 1.37-38: this is vision-knowledge, the rsi's mode, the deep powers of speech are not inferred from usage but beheld by those whose cognition has climbed the very gradient 1.142 mapped. The verse thus locks hierarchy to epistemology: each level of speech is also a station of knowing, and the powers operative at a level are visible only from at or above it.

*Contract.* 3 entities · 3 axioms · 2 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_143`.

*Refuted.* “As the supreme word-station manifests through utterance and returns to unity, it undergoes real change in substance.” (contradicted): Asserts parinamate (real transformation of substance), which the axiom and commentary explicitly deny.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* the referent of tat and the syntax of kriyamanam avasthitam are elliptical; we take 'that' as the param padam persisting through division/non-division, but the pair has also been construed as smṛti-work ('being made, through analysis and synthesis, it stands established'), continuing 1.141's charter, the transcription's ambiguity leaves both live

### 1.144

::: {.deva}
अनादिमव्यवच्छिन्नां श्रुतिमाहुर कर्तृकाम् ।  
शिष्टैर्निबध्यमाना तु न व्यवच्छिद्यते स्मृतिः ॥ १४४ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
anādimavyavacchinnāṃ śrutimāhura kartṛkām |  
śiṣṭairnibadhyamānā tu na vyavacchidyate smṛtiḥ || 144 ||
:::

> They declare śruti to be beginningless, unbroken, without author; smṛti, however, being composed by the cultivated, is yet not discontinued.

The two canons given their formal statuses, completing 1.132's threading of the needle. Śruti: anadi, avyavacchinna, akartrka, beginningless, uninterrupted, authorless: the seed-order of 1.132 in its manifestation, received by seers (1.37), never made. Smṛti: sistair nibadhyamana, *presently being composed*, the participle notably continuous: the cultivated are always writing it, codifying usage and duty age by age (grammar itself among them, 1.141), tu na vyavacchidyate, and yet it too is never severed: authored at every point, unbroken as a stream. The two eternities are thus of different types, śruti eternal in substance (or as the recurrent seed), smṛti eternal only as continuity of composition, the praninam-analogy of 1.28 institutionalized. The pair resolves the tension 1.132 staged (nothing authorless received; something survives all authors): śruti is the surviving seed, smṛti the perpetual re-authoring around it, and the authority of the second derives from unbroken contact with the first (1.7's lingas). The grammarian's own book, this book, is smṛti in exactly this sense: made, dated, mortal, and a link in a chain that is none of those things.

### 1.145

::: {.deva}
अविभागाद्विवृत्तानामभिख्या स्वप्नवच्छ्रुतौ ।  
भावतत्त्वं तु विज्ञाय लिङ्गेभ्यो विहिता स्मृतिः ॥ १४५ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
avibhāgādvivṛttānāmabhikhyā svapnavacchrutau |  
bhāvatattvaṃ tu vijñāya liṅgebhyo vihitā smṛtiḥ || 145 ||
:::

> Of the things unrolled out of the undivided, the naming in śruti is as in a dream; smṛti, however, is framed from the indicatory marks, after knowing the reality of things.

A daring epistemology of revelation, run through the kanda's own cosmology. The bhavas, things, are vivrtta avibhagat, unrolled out of the undivided (the vivarta of 1.1, the descent of 1.112ff.). Their abhikhya, their naming-and-appearing in śruti, is svapnavat: *as in a dream*. The comparison is precise, not deflationary: dream-presentation is immediate, involuntary, whole, the dreamer does not compose the dream but undergoes its self-showing; so the rsi does not construct the Veda but receives the undivided's own self-unrolling into name (1.37's vision, now given its phenomenology, and note that 1.1's Vṛtti had already reached for the dream-analogy for the world-appearance itself). Smṛti by contrast is work: bhava-tattvam vijnaya, having *understood* the reality, lingebhyah vihita, framed from marks, inferentially, deliberately (1.7 verbatim). Revelation is thus dream-like showing; tradition-literature is waking construction upon it, two cognitive modes, not two grades of one. The pairing dignifies both: śruti's authority is its unconstructedness, smṛti's is its method.

*Contract.* 8 entities · 8 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_145`.

*Refuted.* “Since śruti presents things in a dream-like manner, śruti's teachings are illusory and lack true authority.” (contradicted): Misreads svapnavat (dream-like mode of presentation) as implying the non-reality of what is presented, when the commentary explicitly dignifies the immediacy and unconstructedness of śruti's showing.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* svapnavat is the crux: we read it as characterizing the *mode* of śruti's reception (immediate self-presentation, like dream), not its truth-value; a reader who presses the simile toward illusion makes the verse subversive of śruti in a way the whole kanda forbids, yet the choice of so double-edged a comparison is itself surely deliberate
:::


### 1.146

::: {.deva}
कायवाग्बुद्धिविषया ये मलाः समवस्थिताः ।  
चिकित्सालक्षणाध्यात्मशास्त्रैस्तेषां विशुद्धयः ॥ १४६ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
kāyavāgbuddhiviṣayā ye malāḥ samavasthitāḥ |  
cikitsālakṣaṇādhyātmaśāstraisteṣāṃ viśuddhayaḥ || 146 ||
:::

> The impurities that stand settled in body, speech and mind, their purifications are through the sciences of medicine, grammar and the self.

The triad of disciplines, mapped to the triad of faults. Malas, impurities, station themselves in kaya, vac, buddhi, body, speech, intellect: the tradition's standard trio of act-channels (karma is done by body, word and mind). Each has its cathartic science: cikitsa, medicine, for the body's disorders; laksana, 'the science of characteristics', grammar's own honorific title, the discipline of laksya and laksana, usage and rule, for speech; adhyatma-śāstra, the science of the self, for the mind's. The verse institutionalizes 1.14's metaphor (grammar as cikitsita of speech-faults): grammar is not *like* medicine; it occupies the middle seat in a single therapeutic system of the person, flanked by medicine and metaphysics. And the ordering carries the kanda's conviction: speech stands between body and spirit as the hinge-channel, the vag-malas (the apabhramsas about to be discussed, 1.147ff.) are neither physical disease nor mental error but a third species of impurity, with its own pathology, its own materia medica (the sista usage), and its own physicians. Purity of speech is thereby made as real, and as consequential, as health and wisdom.

*Contract.* 7 entities · 8 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_146`.

*Refuted.* “Medicine serves as the therapeutic science for purifying the faults of speech.” (unlicensed): The commentary assigns each science to its proper domain: medicine for body, grammar for speech, and metaphysics for mind.


### 1.147

::: {.deva}
शब्दः संस्कारहीनो यो गौरिति प्रयुयुक्षिते ।  
तमपभ्रंशमिच्छन्ति विशिष्टार्थनिवेशिनम् ॥ १४७ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
śabdaḥ saṃskārahīno yo gauriti prayuyukṣite |  
tamapabhraṃśamicchanti viśiṣṭārthaniveśinam || 147 ||
:::

> The word devoid of the refinement, when 'gauh' is what one intends to employ, that they call apabhramsa, though it settles on the distinguished meaning.

The formal definition of the corrupt form, with its crucial concession renewed. A speaker intends the correct gauh, 'cow'; what leaves him is gavi or goni, saṃskāra-hina, lacking the refinement grammar names (1.131's śabda-saṃskāra, here in its technical sense). That deviation is apabhramsa, the 'fallen-away' form. And the definition's honest clause: visistartha-nivesinam, the fallen form *settles on the distinguished meaning perfectly well*; the hearer gets the cow. Corruption is thus defined against intention and norm, not against communicative success, exactly 1.27's doctrine, now with the mechanism the intervening kanda supplied: the apabhramsa conveys, but conveys *through* the sādhu form it deviates from (as 1.152 will say), like a mispronounced name that reaches its bearer only because the right name is recoverable through it. The dossier 1.147-155 that this opens, the theory of linguistic corruption, is the kanda's most linguistically observant stretch: language change, register, and the sociolinguistics of prestige are all about to appear, inside a normative frame.

*Contract.* 5 entities · 4 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_147`.

*Refuted.* “Since corrupt forms successfully communicate meaning, there is no difference between them and correct forms.” (unlicensed): Ignores the explicit doctrine: corruption is defined by norm and intention, not by communicative success.

*Contested.* prayuyuksite ('when one intends to employ') marks the definition as intention-relative: the same phonetic form is apabhramsa only against a meant sādhu target, which raises, without answering, the status of forms native to speakers who intend nothing else; 1.153-154 return to exactly this edge

### 1.148

::: {.deva}
अस्वगोण्यादयः शब्दाः साधवो विषयान्तरे ।  
निमित्तभेदात् सर्वत्र साधुत्वं च व्यवस्थितम् ॥ १४८ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
asvagoṇyādayaḥ śabdāḥ sādhavo viṣayāntare |  
nimittabhedāt sarvatra sādhutvaṃ ca vyavasthitam || 148 ||
:::

> Words like asva and goni are correct in another domain; correctness everywhere is settled by difference of ground.

The relativity of correctness, stated with complete technical calm. Goni as 'cow' is apabhramsa; goni as 'sack' is sādhu, same phonetic form, different visaya, different verdict. Asva likewise (the traditional pair: as 'non-owner', a perfectly formed negative compound, versus dialectal deformation of asva, 'horse'). Sarvatra, everywhere, sadhutvam nimitta-bhedat vyavasthitam: correctness is fixed by the *ground* of use, the meaning-domain, the derivational warrant, not by the sound in itself. No form is corrupt as acoustics; corruption is a relation between form, meaning and derivational title. The verse quietly explodes any essentialist reading of the mala-doctrine (1.146): the impurity is not *in* the syllables, as a stain is in cloth, it is a failure of entitlement, a form appearing where it holds no warrant, like a true coin passed in the wrong currency. This sharpens rather than softens the norm: since correctness is relational, only the smṛti's map of grounds (1.141) can adjudicate it, and the ear alone never suffices.

*Refuted.* “A word-form is correct or incorrect based entirely on its phonetic shape.” (contradicted): Ignores the relational nature of sadhutvam and its dependence on nimitta-bheda.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* the source's asvagonyadayah we resolve as asva-goni-adayah with the traditional examples; the exact pair matters little, the doctrine of nimitta-bheda is the verse's whole cargo

### 1.149

::: {.deva}
ते साधुष्वनुमानेन प्रत्ययोत्पत्तिहेतवः ।  
तादात्म्यमुपगम्येव शब्दार्थस्य प्रकाशकाः ॥ १४९ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
te sādhuṣvanumānena pratyayotpattihetavaḥ |  
tādātmyamupagamyeva śabdārthasya prakāśakāḥ || 149 ||
:::

> Among correct words, those are causes of the arising of cognition by way of inference; they illuminate the word's meaning as if having attained identity with it.

How the corrupt form manages to mean, the semantics of deviation, first half. Te, the apabhramsas (continuing 1.147-148): amid the sādhus they cause understanding anumanena, by way of inference, the hearer, receiving gavi, recovers gauh as what this speaker, in this mouth-habit, must intend, and the meaning arrives through that recovery; the corrupt form is evidence of the correct one, not its equal. Yet phenomenologically, and the verse is careful to honour this, the deviant form illuminates tadatmyam upagamya iva, *as if* it had become identical with the meaning: the hearer of a familiar solecism feels no inference happening, the sense simply appears. The iva does its usual precise work: seeming-identity, actual mediation. The account is a fine piece of pragmatics, meaning through reconstruction-so-practiced-it-vanishes, and it preserves both halves of 1.27's data: communicative equality in *feel*, normative asymmetry in *mechanism*. The sādhu signifies directly; the apabhramsa signifies through the sādhu, and 1.150 will name the consequence.

*Contract.* 5 entities · 5 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_149`.

*Refuted.* “Corrupt and correct word-forms are equivalent in status and function.” (contradicted): The commentary explicitly states the corrupt form is evidence of the correct one, not equal to it.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* we take te as the apabhramsas working 'among the sādhus' (sadhusu locative of context); the verse is also construed with sādhus as subject, correct words as inferential causes, which inverts the doctrine and fits 1.150 poorly; the passage's flow decides for the first
:::


### 1.150

::: {.deva}
न शिष्टैरनुगम्यन्ते पर्याया इव साधवः ।  
ते यतः स्मृतिशास्त्रेण तस्मात्साक्षादवाचकाः ॥ १५० ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
na śiṣṭairanugamyante paryāyā iva sādhavaḥ |  
te yataḥ smṛtiśāstreṇa tasmātsākṣādavācakāḥ || 150 ||
:::

> The correct forms are not followed by the cultivated as if synonyms of the corrupt; since that is so by the science of tradition, the corrupt are therefore not directly expressive.

The consequence drawn, against the leveller. If corrupt and correct forms both convey the meaning (1.147, 1.149), why not call them paryayas, synonyms, parallel names of one thing, as hasta and kara both name the hand? Because, the verse answers, the sistas do not so treat them: na anugamyante paryaya iva, in cultivated usage the pair never behaves as a synonym-pair does (either member freely chosen, neither corrective of the other); gavi is *corrected to* gauh, never the reverse, and the asymmetry is codified smṛti-sastrena, by the received science (1.141). Therefore: saksat avacakah, the corrupt forms are not *directly* expressive; their conveying is the mediated, inferential kind of 1.149. The argument-form deserves notice: a semantic thesis (direct versus mediated expression) is established from a *sociological* datum (the direction of correction among the cultivated) certified by smṛti, exactly the epistemology 1.27-31 and 1.137 licensed, where the practice of the sistas is the deep evidence and theory follows it. The synonym-test itself is genuinely sharp linguistics: substitutability-without-correction remains a working criterion for synonymy.

*Contract.* 4 entities · 4 axioms · 2 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_150`.

*Refuted.* “Corrupt and correct forms are complete synonyms used interchangeably by cultivated speakers.” (contradicted): Contradicts the core finding that cultivated speakers treat them asymmetrically (gavi corrected to gauh, never reverse).

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* the ablative/instrumental construction yatah smṛti-sastrena is compressed; we read 'since (their non-parity) is so by the smṛti', others read 'since they are not followed (as expressive) *because of* the smṛti', making the smṛti the cause rather than the certifier of the asymmetry; the difference matters for how conventionalist one takes the doctrine to be
:::


### 1.151

::: {.deva}
अम्बाम्बेति यथा बालः शिक्षमाणः प्रभाषते ।  
अव्यक्तं तद्विदां तेन व्यक्ते भवति निश्चयः ॥ १५१ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
ambāmbeti yathā bālaḥ śikṣamāṇaḥ prabhāṣate |  
avyaktaṃ tadvidāṃ tena vyakte bhavati niścayaḥ || 151 ||
:::

> As a child learning to speak utters 'amba, amba' indistinctly, and by it those who know become certain about the distinct form,

The homeliest verse in the kanda, and the model for 1.152. The infant calls amba amba, 'mama', avyaktam, indistinctly: the form is blurred, half-made. Yet tad-vidam, for those who know, the mother, the household, niscayah bhavati vyakte: certainty arises *about the distinct form*; the hearers pass through the blur to the word the child is reaching for, and understanding happens there, at the vyakta, not at the noise. The baby-talk case is perfect for its purpose because the mediation is undisguised: no one thinks the infant's syllables signify in their own right; everyone hears *through* them to amba, inference (1.149) with its workings still visible, before habit has hidden them. It also carries a quiet tenderness of doctrine: the child's fallen form is not censured but *completed* by the knowing hearer, which is what every hearer of apabhramsa does, and what the sista tradition does for the whole speech-community, holding the distinct forms on behalf of tongues still learning. Sanskrit itself, the verse implies, is the language's amba: what the blurred callings mean.

*Contract.* 7 entities · 4 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_151`.

*Refuted.* “The child's indistinct utterance directly and independently signifies the distinct form, without requiring the hearer's inference.” (contradicted): Violates the commentary's explicit teaching that 'no one thinks the infant's syllables signify in their own right'.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* none of substance; the verse is syntactically the clearest in the passage, its work is entirely in the analogy it prepares for 1.152
:::


### 1.152

::: {.deva}
एवं साधौ प्रयोक्तव्ये योऽपभ्रंशः प्रयुज्यते ।  
तेन साधुव्यवहितः कश्चिदर्थोऽभिधीयते ॥ १५२ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
evaṃ sādhau prayoktavye yo'pabhraṃśaḥ prayujyate |  
tena sādhuvyavahitaḥ kaścidartho'bhidhīyate || 152 ||
:::

> So, where the correct form is to be employed, when a corrupt one is employed, some meaning is expressed by it with the correct form intervening.

The apodosis, and the semantics of corruption completed. Evam, as with the child: sadhau prayoktavye, where the sādhu form is the one *to be* employed (the normative participle carrying 1.139's ethics), the apabhramsa that actually issues expresses its meaning sādhu-vyavahitah, with the correct form *intervening*, standing between the corrupt sound and the meaning as the relay through which expression passes. Vyavahita is the doctrine's exact word: not 'replaced by' nor 'associated with' but *interposed*, the hearer's route runs gavi → gauh → cow, the middle term supplied by competence (the mother's knowledge in 1.151, the sista-smṛti at large). This is the final settlement of the problem opened at 1.27: corrupt forms convey (conceded throughout), but their conveying is parasitic, borrowed light, the sādhu form doing the expressing from behind the screen. The theory has real explanatory reach: it predicts that apabhramsa comprehension varies with the hearer's command of the target forms (as it does), and it makes the norm *internal* to the semantics rather than an etiquette laid over it. 1.153 immediately tests the theory at its breaking point: communities where no target form remains.

*Contract.* 3 entities · 4 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_152`.

*Refuted.* “A corrupt form possesses the independent power to express meaning, functioning as the primary expressor without reliance on the correct form.” (contradicted): Wrongly attributes the vachaka (expressor/revealer) relation to apabhramsa.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* kascid arthah, 'some meaning', is lightly hedged, either 'a certain meaning' (the intended one, reached mediately) or a genuine qualification ('some meaning or other', imperfectly determinate); the first fits the child-analogy, the second anticipates 1.153's degraded-transmission case, and the hedge may be deliberately open
:::


### 1.153

::: {.deva}
पारम्पर्यादपभ्रंशा विगुणेष्वभिधातृषु ।  
प्रसिद्धिमागता येषु तेषां साधुरवाचकः ॥ १५३ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
pāramparyādapabhraṃśā viguṇeṣvabhidhātṛṣu |  
prasiddhimāgatā yeṣu teṣāṃ sādhuravācakaḥ || 153 ||
:::

> When, through transmission among defective speakers, corrupt forms have attained establishment, for those speakers it is the correct form that is non-expressive.

The theory driven honestly into its hardest case, and yielding to it. Paramparyat: by transmission, generation upon generation, among vigunesu abhidhatrsu, speakers gone defective (communities severed from the sista line). There the apabhramsas prasiddhim agatah, have attained establishment, become the *current* forms, rudhi (1.128) now running entirely on the fallen side. And the verse does not flinch from the consequence: tesam, for those speakers, sadhur avacakah, it is the *correct* form that fails to express. The relay of 1.152 has inverted: where gavi is what everyone says and gauh is heard as strange noise, the mediation runs the other way or not at all; expressiveness follows establishment, and establishment follows transmission, wherever transmission goes. This is a remarkable concession for a normative grammarian, it grants the Prakrits their full semantic citizenship within their own communities and concedes that sadhutva confers no magical reach beyond the community that sustains it. What survives the concession is only, but exactly, the dharma-claim (1.27, 1.139): the established corrupt form expresses, in its own domain, as well as Sanskrit ever did, what it does not do is bear the unseen fruit. The norm has retreated, under pressure of honest linguistics, to the one ground (adrsta) where observation cannot follow to contest it.

*Contract.* 7 entities · 6 axioms · 2 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_153`.

*Refuted.* “The correct form remains expressive and authoritative regardless of speaker community.” (contradicted): Violates the core doctrine that expressiveness follows establishment and community.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* none syntactically; doctrinally, whether 1.153 describes a regrettable pathology or simply *how language communities work* is the live question, the verse's own even tone supports the descriptive reading, and 1.154 shows the school dividing over precisely this
:::


### 1.154

::: {.deva}
दैवी वाग्व्यतिकीर्णेयमशक्तैरभिधातृभिः ।  
अनित्यदर्शिनां त्वस्मिन् वादे बुद्धिविपर्ययः ॥ १५४ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
daivī vāgvyatikīrṇeyamaśaktairabhidhātṛbhiḥ |  
anityadarśināṃ tvasmin vāde buddhiviparyayaḥ || 154 ||
:::

> This divine speech has been overspread by incapable speakers; among the seers of non-eternality, in this matter, the judgement is the reverse.

The two ideologies of language change, set face to face. First the school's own telling: daivi vak, divine speech, the Sanskrit of the seed-tradition, vyatikirnā, overspread, scattered through, asaktaih abhidhatrbhih, by speakers without capacity (the vigunas of 1.153): change as corruption, history as the silting of a pure source. Then, with striking evenhandedness, the rival telling: anitya-darsinam, for the seers of non-eternality, those whose darśana makes impermanence fundamental (the Buddhists above all, though any anti-eternalist qualifies), asmin vade buddhi-viparyayah: in this dispute their judgement is the exact reverse. On their view the current speech of peoples is the natural datum and 'divine Sanskrit' the artifact, a fixed, frozen elaboration raised out of living vernaculars; the direction of derivation flips with the metaphysics. Bhartṛhari states the inversion without refuting it, consistent with 1.140's concession that about any āgama the contrary can be alleged, and the honesty is structural: he has already granted (1.153) everything the rival needs descriptively; what separates the schools is not the linguistics but the eternality-metaphysics, and *that*, by his own epistemology, argument alone will not settle (1.34). The verse is perhaps the earliest clear statement that the Sanskrit-Prakrit priority dispute is theory-laden all the way down.

*Contract.* 6 entities · 4 axioms · 0 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_154`.

*Refuted.* “The seers of non-eternality have a reversed and therefore false judgement about language.” (unlicensed): Projects refutation onto the text when the commentary explicitly states Bhartṛhari does not refute the opposite view.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* vyatikirna we render 'overspread/scattered-through'; it admits 'mixed, adulterated', the image of seed-crop overgrown; the identification of the anitya-darsins with Buddhists specifically is traditional and probable but the karika names no school
:::


### 1.155

::: {.deva}
उभयेषामविच्छेदादन्यशब्दविवक्षया ।  
योन्यः प्रयुज्यते शब्दो न सोऽर्थस्याभिधायकः ॥ १५५ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
ubhayeṣāmavicchedādanyaśabdavivakṣayā |  
yonyaḥ prayujyate śabdo na so'rthasyābhidhāyakaḥ || 155 ||
:::

> Because both streams are unbroken, when a word is employed with the intention of another word, that word is not the expressor of the meaning.

The kanda's final karika in this recension of the text, resolving 1.154's standoff at the level of practice. Ubhayesam avicchedat, both lines, sādhu and apabhramsa, sista-transmission and vernacular transmission, are *unbroken*: each has the continuity that 1.31 and 1.141 made the touchstone of standing; neither can be dismissed as a mere lapse of the other (this much is 1.153-154 consolidated). The discrimination therefore falls back, as at 1.147, on intention within employment: anya-śabda-vivaksaya yo 'nyah prayujyate sabdah, when one word is employed *with the intention of another word* (gavi uttered reaching for gauh, or indeed the reverse), na so 'rthasya abhidhayakah: that uttered word is not the expressor; the intended word is, through it (1.152). The doctrine's last word is thus neither the triumph of Sanskrit nor its relativization, but a criterion indifferent to which stream is 'first': expression belongs to the form the speaker's vivaksa actually deploys, and forms reached-through are conduits, not expressors. That this closes the Brahmakanda is fitting beyond its topic: the kanda began with the undivided word appearing as many (1.1) and ends with the everyday case of one word appearing through another, the whole metaphysics recapitulated in a grammarian's scruple about who, in the utterance, is really doing the meaning.

*Contract.* 4 entities · 3 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_155`.

*Refuted.* “When a word is employed with the intention of another word, that employed word expresses the meaning directly.” (contradicted): Directly contradicts the verse's explicit claim that the employed word is not the expressor.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* the verse generalizes 1.147's intention-relativity, and its scope is debated: read narrowly it restates the apabhramsa doctrine; read broadly it covers *any* employment-through (citation, metalepsis, even the dhvani-sphoṭa relation itself), the placement as kanda-close invites the broad reading, and we incline to it; note also that the karika-count of Kanda I varies between recensions, wisdomlib's text ending at 155 while other editions carry additional closing verses
:::
