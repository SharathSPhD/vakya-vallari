# 21 · The Necessity of Tradition

*Verses 1.132 to 1.141 · 10 units*

The last chapter made the word constitutive of cognition. This one asks how such word-knowledge ever reaches anyone, and its answer is tradition. The movement 1.132 to 1.141 argues that āgama, received teaching, is not a convenience but a necessity. Knowledge is not innate, so it must be taught. Reason cannot stand on its own feet, so it must be anchored. And the practices that carry dharma outlast every doctrine and every named author, so their ground must be something steadier than argument. This is a return to the philosophy of tradition begun far back at 1.27 to 1.43, now taken up again at a deeper station and with a puzzle set on its face.

The keystone is 1.132, and the puzzle is sharp: nobody accepts a tradition to be without an author, yet the Veda is held to be authorless. The resolution is the registry split that the featured section works out. The word veda carries two sorts. Elsewhere in the corpus it names the recited body of text, the āgama, which is composed by someone in each world-age and perishes when that age dissolves. Here it names the bīja, the eternal seed-order, the vyavasthā that persists through the dissolution between ages and out of which the seers re-establish the triple Veda by seeing it again, not by composing it. One term, two sorts, and the whole argument of the chapter rests on keeping them apart. From that keystone the movement fans out: 1.133 gives the empirical companion, that when doctrines have set and their authors are gone the world still does not transgress the rite; 1.134 poses a dilemma to the naturalist about knowledge, each horn closing toward tradition; 1.135 to 1.137 rehabilitate reasoning and then sharply subordinate it; 1.138 and 1.139 argue from the fixed powers of words to a practical ethics of correct speech; 1.140 makes a startling concession; and 1.141 defines the grammatical tradition itself.

Four cards reward a slow read. Slow down at 1.133, because it states the asymmetry the whole chapter defends in one image: the doctrines set like stars and their authors die, but the rite declared by śruti and smṛti is not transgressed. Slow down at 1.135, because it is the kāṇḍa's last and most generous word on reason, calling tarka an eye for those who cannot see, on the one condition that it not conflict with Veda and śāstra. Slow down at 1.137, because that condition is here made absolute: the power belongs to words themselves, and reasoning has authority only while it follows the words, and none standing apart from the traditions. And slow down at 1.140, because it is a concession of disarming honesty placed exactly where the argument is strongest: everyone reaches the objects of unseen fruit through tradition, and yet about tradition the contrary can everywhere be alleged.

In the cards, watch the registry do its work. Once veda is split, the failure modes almost sort themselves. A reading that makes the āgama itself eternal fails as contradicted, because it confuses the perishable manifestation with the imperishable seed (1.132). A reading that makes the seers composers rather than perceivers fails the same way, because the verse has them see what already is. And a reading that demands an author for the eternal seed too, extending to the bīja the need for a kartṛ that belongs only to the āgama, fails as unlicensed, an over-application of a rule outside its sort. The pattern continues past the keystone. At 1.134 the claim that knowledge is innate and natural fails as contradicted, because it makes the śāstras purposeless. At 1.135 the claim that reasoning alone suffices fails as unlicensed, because it drops the non-conflict condition the verse builds in. At 1.137 a reading that makes reason and word-power equal authorities fails as unlicensed, missing the radical subordination. At 1.138 the claim that words are merely conventional signs fails as contradicted. And at 1.141 a reading that makes grammar the study of meaning fails as unlicensed, because the verse fixes its object as the knowledge of correctness, not of meaning.

Two kinds of contested note run through this chapter, and both repay attention. The first is doctrinal. At 1.132 the opening line can be read as an opponent's premise that the seed-doctrine then answers, or as Bhartṛhari's own concession on the way to it, and the dialectical cut differs between readers. At 1.137 the verse can be read as subordinating personal reasoning to word-grounded principle, our reading, or as identifying the two, a more charitable unity that reaches the same practical upshot: no freestanding authority for tarka. The second kind is textual. This stretch of the transcription is unusually corrupt, and the edition flags each fault in the open rather than smoothing it over: nivandhanam read as nibandhanam at 1.134, the damaged sarvo drpha- restored to objects of unseen fruit at 1.140, yataśaktayaḥ read as fixed powers at 1.138. In every case the edition names the source's fault, states its emendation, and reads through it.

The chapter ends quietly. After the grand claims about seed and author and reason, 1.141 defines the grammatical tradition as that whose object is the knowledge of correctness, grounded in the unbroken remembrance of the cultivated. That plain definition is the bridge to the last movement of the book. Having secured why correctness of forms matters and where its authority lies, the text is ready to climb from the correct form up through the levels of speech to the summit that stands above them all.

## Featured verse: 1.132 in slow motion

The verse on the eternal seed-order grounds the registry's veda split: the recited corpus elsewhere, the aeon-persisting bīja here. One term, two sorts, each with its one-sentence justification. The gate would have flagged either use as drift without the ruling.

**Axioms, each with its verbatim license.**

- identity `bija = vyavastha`: “the vyavastha, the seed-order”
- relation `asraya(agama → kartr)`: “every current of teaching is received as *someone's*”
- relation `vivartate(bija → veda)`: “from which the triple Veda re-establishes itself”
- relation `drś(rshi → veda)`: “the rsis who *see* it again”
- predication `akartr(veda)`: “authorless as the seed”
- predication `eternal(vyavastha)`: “what is eternal is not any authored text-body but the vyavastha, the seed-order”
- predication `eternal(bija)`: “when all traditions pass away (in the dissolution between world-ages, on the cosmology in play), a seed persists”

**Denials.**

- `eternal(agama)`: “when all traditions pass away”
- `karta(rshi → veda)`: “not composers”

**Rejected readings and their compiled fates.**

- “A teaching-tradition, once established, persists eternally and requires a constant sustaining agent.” → **contradicted**. Confuses the temporal manifestation (āgama, which the verse says perish) with the eternal essence (the seed). Āgama is established in each age by a kartṛ but doesn't persist eternally.
- “The wise seers (rṣis) compose the Veda anew in each age according to their insights.” → **contradicted**. Misrepresents the role of rṣis as creators/composers rather than perceivers. The verse says rṣis 'see' the eternal Veda, meaning they perceive what already exists (the eternal seed-order), not invent new teachings.
- “The eternal seed-order also requires an originating agent (Ishvara/God) to establish it.” → **unlicensed**. Incorrectly extends the need for a kartṛ (which applies to the temporal manifestation āgama) to the eternal seed (bīja). The verse distinguishes: āgama needs a kartṛ; the seed is self-persisting and eternal.

**Theorems in `V1_132.lean`.** `accepted_adequate`, `eternal_agama_inadequate`, `rsi_composers_inadequate`, `seed_needs_agent_inadequate`. All close by `decide`; the build carries zero `sorry`.


## The verses

### 1.132

::: {.deva}
न जात्वकर्तृकं कश्चिदागमं प्रतिपद्यते ।  
बीजं सर्वागमापाये त्रय्येवातो व्यवस्थिता ॥ १३२ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
na jātvakartṛkaṃ kaścidāgamaṃ pratipadyate |  
bījaṃ sarvāgamāpāye trayyevāto vyavasthitā || 132 ||
:::

> Never does anyone accept a tradition to be without an author; when all traditions perish, the seed remains, hence the triple Veda stands established.

A return to the philosophy of tradition (1.27-43) at a deeper station, with a puzzle set on its face. First line: no āgama circulates as ownerless, every current of teaching is received as *someone's*, akartrkam pratipadyate no one. Second line: sarvagamapaye bijam, when all traditions pass away (in the dissolution between world-ages, on the cosmology in play), a seed persists, from which the triple Veda re-establishes itself (trayy evato vyavasthita). The two lines look opposed, everything authored, yet something survives all authors, and the resolution is the doctrine of 1.28 writ cosmic: what is eternal is not any authored text-body but the vyavastha, the seed-order that re-flowers identically each age; the 'authors' each cycle are the rsis who *see* it again (1.37), not composers. The verse thereby threads between Mimamsa (authorless Veda) and Nyaya (authored, by God): authored in every manifestation, authorless as the seed. Bīja completes the metaphor-chain of 1.84 and 1.111, trace-seed in the hearer, cloud-seed in the atoms, now world-seed across dissolutions: at every scale, the word persists as latency between manifestations.

*Contract.* 6 entities · 7 axioms · 2 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_132`.

*Refuted.* “A teaching-tradition, once established, persists eternally and requires a constant sustaining agent.” (contradicted): Confuses the temporal manifestation (āgama, which the verse says perish) with the eternal essence (the seed).

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* the first line can be read as the opponent's premise (nobody accepts an authorless tradition, so how is the Veda authorless?) answered by the second, or as Bhartṛhari's own concession en route to the seed-doctrine; we take the latter, but the dialectical cut differs between readers
:::


### 1.133

::: {.deva}
अस्तं यातेषु वादेषु कर्तृष्वन्येष्वसत्स्वपि ।  
श्रुतिस्मृत्युदितं कर्म लोको न व्यतिवर्तते ॥ १३३ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
astaṃ yāteṣu vādeṣu kartṛṣvanyeṣvasatsvapi |  
śrutismṛtyuditaṃ karma loko na vyativartate || 133 ||
:::

> When the doctrines have set, and other authors are no more, the world does not transgress the rite declared by śruti and smṛti.

The empirical companion to 1.132's cosmology. Vadas, the philosophical systems, with their founders and their fortunes, asta yatesu, set like stars; their authors pass, their schools scatter. Yet the karma declared in śruti and smṛti, the ritual and practical order, lokah na vyativartate, the world does not step across it; the agnihotra outlives every philosophy of the agnihotra. The observation is sociological and, within its horizon, accurate: doctrines are mortal in a way that rites are not, argument-driven allegiance decaying faster than practice-driven transmission. Its argumentative work: to display, within recorded history, the same asymmetry 1.132 asserted across cosmic dissolutions, teachings-with-authors perish, the practice-bearing core persists, so that the seed-doctrine is not exotic metaphysics but the long shape of ordinary cultural time. It also silently continues 1.30-31: the immunity of the unbroken practice to tarka is here shown, not argued, the vadas that would have judged it are the ones that have set.

*Contract.* 6 entities · 6 axioms · 2 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_133`.

*Refuted.* “When doctrines and their authors pass away, the world likewise abandons its practices.” (contradicted): Inverts the asymmetry: the commentary insists doctrines are mortal but rites are not.


### 1.134

::: {.deva}
ज्ञाने स्वाभाविके नार्थः शास्त्रैः कश्चन विद्यते ।  
धर्मो ज्ञानस्य हेतुश्वेत्तस्याम्नायो निवन्धनम् ॥ १३४ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
jñāne svābhāvike nārthaḥ śāstraiḥ kaścana vidyate |  
dharmo jñānasya hetuśvettasyāmnāyo nivandhanam || 134 ||
:::

> If knowledge were natural, there would be no purpose whatever in the śāstras; if dharma is the cause of knowledge, then tradition is its ground.

A dilemma posed to the naturalist about knowledge, each horn closing toward tradition. Suppose jñāna svabhavika, cognition arises naturally, needing no instruction: then the śāstras are pointless; yet no party to the debate, least of all the naturalist writing books, treats them so, reductio by performative fact. Alternatively, grant what the tradition holds: that dharma conditions knowing, the knower's moral-ritual formation is causally upstream of his cognitive reach (the yogic epistemology of 1.37 presupposed exactly this). Then, since dharma is not established by unaided reason (1.30), amnayo nibandhanam: tradition is knowledge's ground, at one remove. Either the śāstras are idle (which practice refutes) or they are load-bearing via dharma (which makes āgama foundational). The verse formalizes what the kanda has assumed since 1.14 called grammar a purifier: disciplines do not merely inform; they *qualify the knower*, and any epistemology that ignores the knower's formation has, on this view, mistaken its subject.

*Contract.* 6 entities · 3 axioms · 2 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_134`.

*Refuted.* “Knowledge is innate and arises naturally without instruction or tradition.” (contradicted): Asserts that jñāna is svābhāvika (naturally given), which leads to the false consequence that śāstras are purposeless.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* the second pada's syntax (dharmo jnanasya hetus cet...) can be read as a genuine conditional the author endorses, or as continuing the opponent's alternatives; the wisdomlib transcription's nivandhanam for nibandhanam is the v/b alternation common in the source and we read nibandhanam
:::


### 1.135

::: {.deva}
वेदशास्त्राविरोधी च तर्कश्चक्षुरपश्यताम् ।  
रूपमात्राद्धि वाक्यार्थः केवलान्नावतिष्ठते ॥ १३५ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
vedaśāstrāvirodhī ca tarkaścakṣurapaśyatām |  
rūpamātrāddhi vākyārthaḥ kevalānnāvatiṣṭhate || 135 ||
:::

> And reasoning that does not conflict with Veda and śāstra is an eye for those who do not see; for from the mere form alone the meaning of a sentence does not stand settled.

The rehabilitation of tarka, the kanda's last word on reason, and a deliberately generous one. After the demolition of 1.32-42, reasoning returns with a licensed office: veda-śāstra-avirodhi tarkah, reasoning that keeps concord with Veda and śāstra, is caksur apasyatam, an *eye for the sightless*, for those without the rsi's direct vision (1.37-38), which is to say for everyone now. And the second line gives the reason tradition itself requires this eye: rūpa-matrat kevalat, from the bare form alone, vakyarthah na avatisthate, the meaning of a sentence does not settle. Texts do not interpret themselves; the received word, precisely because it is received as *form*, demands construal, and construal is reasoning's proper province. So the settlement: tarka is blind as a foundation (1.30) and indispensable as an instrument, sovereign nowhere, employed everywhere, its concord with the tradition being the condition of its safe use, as the frame of a lens is to its glass. That sentence-meaning outruns sentence-form is, moreover, no small aside: it is the thesis-seed of the entire second kanda, where what a vākya means will be argued to be an undivided pratibhā that the parsed form merely occasions. The kanda's epistemological ledger thus closes balanced: vision above, tradition around, reason within.

*Contract.* 6 entities · 4 axioms · 2 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_135`.

*Refuted.* “Reasoning alone, without reference to Veda and śāstra, is sufficient to establish sentence-meaning.” (unlicensed): The verse explicitly conditions tarka's validity on non-conflict with Veda and śāstra (vedaśāstrāvirodhī).


### 1.136

::: {.deva}
सतोऽविवक्षा पारार्थ्यं व्यक्तिरर्थस्य लैङ्गिकी ।  
इति न्यायो बहुविधस्तर्केण प्रविभज्यते ॥ १३६ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
sato'vivakṣā pārārthyaṃ vyaktirarthasya laiṅgikī |  
iti nyāyo bahuvidhastarkeṇa pravibhajyate || 136 ||
:::

> Non-intention of what is present, subservience to another, indication of a meaning through a mark, such manifold interpretive principle is articulated by reasoning.

What the licensed tarka of 1.135 actually does, shown by its instruments. The verse names three canons from the interpreter's working kit: sato 'vivaksa, a real feature of the situation may be *unintended*, present but not meant (the speaker mentions the man's staff without meaning staffness); pararthya, an element may stand wholly in service of another, its sense exhausted in subordination (the Mimamsa principle governing subsidiary and principal); vyaktir laingiki, a meaning may be delivered only through its linga, its indicatory mark, never said outright. Each canon adjudicates precisely the gap 1.135 identified between form and meaning: form alone cannot tell you what was not-intended, what is subordinate, what is marked-at, judgement must. Iti nyayo bahuvidhah: and such maxims are many; tarkena pravibhajyate, reasoning is what articulates and applies them. The verse is a window onto the actual exegetical culture, Mimamsa's interpretive jurisprudence, that the kanda's abstractions have presupposed throughout: 'tradition needs an eye' is no metaphor; here are the lens-grindings.

*Contract.* 7 entities · 5 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_136`.

*Refuted.* “Form alone exhaustively determines meaning through intention, subordination, and marking.” (contradicted): Asserts that form can directly establish artha without judgment, contradicting the verse's core point that gap between form and meaning requires tarka (reasoning) to bridge it.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* the three items are compressed to the point of riddle and translators divide sharply; we take them as three named canons of interpretation (unintendedness of the actual, subservience, mark-borne indication), but each compound admits alternative resolution, e.g. sato 'vivaksa as 'non-assertion of the existent' in the ontological sense, and the verse may be quoting a list whose original context is lost
:::


### 1.137

::: {.deva}
शब्दानामेव सा शक्तिस्तर्को यः पुरुषाश्रयः ।  
स शब्दानुगतो न्यायोऽनागमेष्वनिवन्धनः ॥ १३७ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
śabdānāmeva sā śaktistarko yaḥ puruṣāśrayaḥ |  
sa śabdānugato nyāyo'nāgameṣvanivandhanaḥ || 137 ||
:::

> That power belongs to words themselves; the reasoning that rests on a person, when it follows the words, is interpretive principle, apart from the traditions it is without ground.

The settlement of reason's status in one careful sentence. The interpretive maxims of 1.136 work, but their śakti, their authority, sabdanam eva: belongs to the words themselves, to the structure of the received language they explicate. Tarka as it lives in a reasoner, purusasraya, person-based, the wit of Devadatta, is authoritative only śabda-anugata, when it follows the words: tracking, subserving, drawing out. Cut loose, anagamesu, where there is no received text to follow, it is anibandhana, groundless: the same activity that was an eye within tradition (1.135) is a wanderer without it. This resolves the apparent zigzag of the kanda's epistemology (reason demolished 1.32-42, licensed 1.135-136): there were never two doctrines, only one, reasoning is real as an *instrument of the word* and nothing as an independent source. The formulation is also quietly self-referential: the Vākyapadīya itself, bristling with arguments, claims authority not as one man's tarka but as reasoning anugata to the grammarians' āgama, the book practices the epistemology it preaches.

*Contract.* 6 entities · 4 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_137`.

*Refuted.* “The personal reasoning power and word-power are essentially equivalent sources of interpretive authority; both work together as equal instruments.” (unlicensed): This misses the verse's radical subordination: śakti belongs to words; tarka has authority only when following words.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* we read the verse as *subordinating* personal reasoning to word-grounded principle; it can also be read as *identifying* them ('the nyaya just is that word-power as refracted through persons'), a more charitable unity, the practical upshot (no freestanding authority for tarka) is the same
:::


### 1.138

::: {.deva}
रूपादयो यथा दृष्टाः प्रत्यर्थं यतशक्तयः ।  
शब्दास्तथैव दृश्यन्ते विषापहरणादिषु ॥ १३८ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
rūpādayo yathā dṛṣṭāḥ pratyarthaṃ yataśaktayaḥ |  
śabdāstathaiva dṛśyante viṣāpaharaṇādiṣu || 138 ||
:::

> As colour and the rest are seen to have their powers fixed with respect to each object, just so are words seen to have theirs, in the removal of poison and the like.

Evidence that words have real, object-fixed powers, from the mantra. The analogy's base: sensible qualities have yata-śakti, determinate powers indexed to their bearers (this herb's colour signals this virtue; fire's touch burns and water's cools, invariably). The claim: words exhibit the same fixity, and the evidence offered is visapaharana-adi, the removal of poison and such: the practice of mantra-healing, where *this* formula and no paraphrase of it counteracts *this* venom. Whatever a modern reader makes of the practice, its logic is the argument's point: mantra-efficacy is form-bound, substitute a synonym and the power is gone, which is inexplicable if words are arbitrary labels whose whole being is conventional reference, but exactly what one expects if particular word-forms carry particular śaktis as natively as colours carry theirs. The verse recruits the tradition's applied linguistics (the whole culture of mantra-śāstra) as empirical support for the ontology of 1.13 and 1.128: words are powers before they are signs.

*Contract.* 5 entities · 4 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_138`.

*Refuted.* “Words are conventional signs whose meaning depends entirely on social agreement and shared reference conventions, possessing no intrinsic power of their own.” (contradicted): Treats śabda as merely_conventional, contradicting the verse's core claim that words exhibit the same fixed powers as sensible qualities do.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* the wisdomlib transcription's yatasaktayah we read as yata-śakti ('fixed powers', as at 1.6); the sceptical reader will note the argument is only as strong as the mantra-datum, which Bhartṛhari's audience granted and ours may not, the karika's own force is conditional on that concession
:::


### 1.139

::: {.deva}
यथैषां तत्र सामर्थ्यं धर्मेऽप्येवं प्रतीयताम् ।  
साधूनां साधुभिस्तस्माद्वाच्यमभ्युदयार्थिनाम् ॥ १३९ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
yathaiṣāṃ tatra sāmarthyaṃ dharme'pyevaṃ pratīyatām |  
sādhūnāṃ sādhubhistasmādvācyamabhyudayārthinām || 139 ||
:::

> As their capacity is acknowledged there, so let it be acknowledged with respect to dharma as well; therefore what is correct is to be spoken by the correct-speaking who seek felicity.

The inference from 1.138 drawn, and the kanda's practical ethics stated. If words have real form-bound powers in the visible domain (poison-cure), yatha esam tatra samarthyam, then evam dharme 'pi pratiyatam, let the same be acknowledged where the fruit is unseen: in dharma. The step is the standard extension from drsta to adrsta efficacy that ritual reasoning always makes (1.7's dual purposes), here applied to speech-forms themselves. Conclusion, marked tasmat: sadhunam sadhubhir vacyam abhyudaya-arthinam, correct words are to be spoken, by the cultivated, for those (or: by those) who seek abhyudaya, felicity, the rising good. The verse completes the arc opened at 1.27: there, correct and corrupt forms conveyed meaning equally and correctness was dharma, on authority; now the doctrine has its mechanism (words as powers, 1.138) and its imperative (speak the sādhu form, the power is in the form, so the form is the practice). Grammar's rules are hereby cashed as ethics: usage is conduct, and conjugation, rightly done, is a small continuous sacrifice.

*Refuted.* “Correct and incorrect word-forms are equally efficacious in pursuing dharma.” (unlicensed): Denies that samarthya (capacity/power) is form-bound.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* sadhubhih can be instrumental of agent ('by the cultivated') or of accompaniment/means, and abhyudaya-arthinam genitive plural can attach to the speakers or beneficiaries; the injunction's sense, right forms for the sake of felicity, is stable across the parsings

### 1.140

::: {.deva}
सर्वोऽदृफलानर्थानागमात् प्रतिपद्यते ।  
विपरीतं च सर्वत्र शक्यते वक्तुमागमे ॥ १४० ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
sarvo'dṛphalānarthānāgamāt pratipadyate |  
viparītaṃ ca sarvatra śakyate vaktumāgame || 140 ||
:::

> Everyone arrives at objects whose fruit is unseen through tradition; and about tradition, the contrary can everywhere be alleged.

A concession of disarming honesty, placed exactly where the argument is strongest. First line: adrsta-phala objects, dharma, the felicities of 1.139, everything whose fruit no one living has seen, are reached agamat, through tradition, by *everyone*: there is no other road (1.30 again, now as sociology). Second line: viparitam ca sarvatra sakyate vaktum agame, and about any tradition, the opposite can always be said. Counter-traditions exist; every āgama has a rival alleging its inversion; no received teaching is immune to contradiction by another received teaching. Bhartṛhari does not blink the consequence: tradition-dependence cannot be *demonstrated* into safety; the sceptic can always speak. What follows for him is not suspension but the practical resolution already given, one stands in the unbroken usage of one's sistas (1.31, 1.141 next) because standing somewhere is not optional and brokenness is the only disqualifier available. The verse is the kanda's moment of epistemic candour: the ground of practice is firm enough to live on and too contested to prove, and the philosophy of tradition must be written for exactly that terrain.

*Refuted.* “Tradition provides certain and conclusive knowledge of unseen objects, and no opposing view can be validly stated.” (contradicted): Directly contradicts the niscaya denial and the virodha axiom.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* the transcription sarvo 'drphalanarthan is corrupt at drpha-; context and the parallel constructions make adrsta-phalan arthan ('objects of unseen fruit') the evident reading, and we translate so while noting the source's fault

### 1.141

::: {.deva}
साधुत्वज्ञानविषया सैषा व्याकरणस्मृतिः ।  
अविच्छेदेन शिष्टानामिदं स्मृतिनिबन्धनम् ॥ १४१ ॥

> This grammatical tradition is that whose object is the knowledge of correctness; it is grounded in the unbroken remembrance of the cultivated.

The kanda's definition of its own discipline, quiet after the grand claims. Vyākaraṇa-smṛti, grammar as a *smṛti*, a remembrance-tradition, not a śruti: the classification is itself doctrine (1.7 made smṛtis derivative of the Veda via lingas; grammar is such a derivative, the sistas' codified memory). Its visaya: sadhutva-jñāna, knowledge of which forms are correct, not meaning (which corrupt forms convey equally, 1.27), not communication, but correctness as such, the dharma-bearing property. Its ground: avicchedena sistanam, the *unbrokenness* of the cultivated line, idam smṛti-nibandhanam, this is what fastens the remembrance. Every term has been earned: aviccheda from 1.31 and 1.41 (the immunity of the unbroken), sista from 1.27 (usage of the learned as criterion), nibandhana as the kanda's own word for grounding since 1.13. The verse is the discipline's charter written in its own vocabulary, and its modesty matters: after word-Brahman and cosmic seeds, grammar's proximate authority is simply that the chain of cultivated speakers never broke.
