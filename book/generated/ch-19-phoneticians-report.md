# 19 · The Phoneticians' Report

*Verses 1.102 to 1.122 · 18 units*

For six chapters the walk has been building. The sphoṭa doctrine was raised, the phoneme, word, and sentence relations were tested, and each verse licensed some readings and refuted others. This movement, 1.102 to 1.122, does something the walk has not done before. It steps back and reports. From 1.102 the kārikās turn doxographic. They survey what rival schools have said about how a sound is made and what, at bottom, becomes the word. Bhartṛhari does not argue here so much as catalogue. The work of the movement is to set the competing accounts side by side, let each state its physics in its own terms, and only at the very end fold the whole survey back into his own cosmology.

The report comes in three panels. The first, 1.102 to 1.106, belongs to the phoneticians. In their usage sphoṭa names the first-produced sound, and the dhvanis are the sounds born from it. Around that naming the panel builds a small physics: the time of the sphoṭa does not vary with loudness though the after-train does (1.103), the bare sound carries from afar like a lamp's glow even where the word is not understood (1.104), the striking of the substance apportions even vowel-length (1.105), and one sound kindles the next as flame from flame (1.106). The second panel, 1.107 to 1.111, widens the question to first principles: by some it is air, by some the atoms, by some cognition that is held to become word, and the divergence settles nowhere (1.107). The air doctrine gets a full mechanist's statement (1.108 to 1.109), and the atomist refinement answers it, its word-atoms gathering like clouds that have edges only at a distance and no oneness for the hearer's single grasp (1.110 to 1.111). The third panel changes temperature. At 1.112 the anonymous reportage gives way to vivarta vocabulary that is Bhartṛhari's own, and the doxography dissolves into the doctrine. The descent of speech then runs through breath, mind, and fire down into the phonemes (1.115), behind which stands a source that is never spent (1.116); 1.117 gathers the stations into one power, 1.118 folds the linguistics back into the cosmology, and after arguments from music, scripture, and the cradle (1.119 to 1.121), 1.122 seals the circle by making even the motor acts of speech depend on a conceiving in word.

Readers arriving here hold a tested instrument and a long stretch of doctrine already walked. What this movement adds is a hazard. It is the most quoted-but-not-endorsed passage in the kāṇḍa, and the standing danger, for a human reader and for a machine alike, is to take the reportage for the thesis. The apparatus meets that danger with a registry ruling, which the featured section on 1.102 lays out. For the span 1.102 to 1.106, sphoṭa is quarantined to its phonetician sense, the first-produced sound, acoustic and produced. It is not allowed to mean the partless meaning-bearer of Bhartṛhari's own doctrine. One term carries two sorts, held apart by the registry so that the report can never contaminate the teaching. Both sides of that wall keep their theorems green.

Four cards reward a slow read. Slow down at 1.104, because it is the panel's one appeal to plain experience: a bell struck nearby shows the difference the theory needs, and the sound of a word reaches the ear from a distance at which its meaning does not. Slow down at 1.107, because a single line, that the divergence of views is unsettled, is the whole doxography's honest hinge, and the rejected reading that commits to air as the sole substrate fails against it. Slow down at 1.112 and 1.113, because this is where the survey stops being a survey: the inner knower turns itself into wordhood by vivarta, not by real change, and the vocabulary that was borrowed from opponents is now unmistakably the author's. And slow down at 1.118, because it is the crescendo, where the power residing in words is named as what binds the universe, and the undivided self is said only to appear in the form of difference.

In the cards, watch the verdict split. The commonest failure mode across the physics panels is reversal. Again and again a rejected reading turns the acoustic relation around: it makes the sphoṭa-time vary while the after-train stays fixed (1.103), assigns vowel-length to the free decay rather than the driven phase (1.105), or makes the organ, not the source, the ongoing producer (1.106). Each such reading contradicts a claim the commentary states outright, so it fails as contradicted, not merely unlicensed. The other pole is the sort error. At 1.114 a reading treats vāyu as ordinary physical substance, a manifestation-sort thing, when the passage means a subtle power, and that mismatch fails as unlicensed. At 1.120 a reading takes parināma, real transformation, as Bhartṛhari's own position rather than as scriptural shorthand for what is strictly vivarta, and fails as contradicted. Two verdicts, two kinds of wrongness, and this chapter drills both.

Watch the contested boxes above all, because this chapter carries more of them than any other in the walk. Nearly every card has one, and they cluster on a single seam: how far the reported terminology of 1.102 still governs the verse in hand. Is the sphoṭa-time of 1.103 the duration of a produced sound, or a restatement of the invariance thesis from 1.75? Does the flame simile of 1.106 fit production or manifestation? Do 1.112 and 1.113 still belong to the doxography of 1.107, or do they mark the author resuming his own voice? The edition does not paper over these seams. It marks each one, states which side it took, and records the doubt. That habit of visible doubt is the part of the apparatus most translations leave out, and here it does its heaviest work.

The report ends by turning inward. Having surveyed how sound is made, the text asks next what cognition is made of, and finds the word already inside it. That is the thesis of the chapter to come.

## Featured verse: 1.102 in slow motion

From here to 1.106 the text reports the phoneticians' rival terminology, in which sphoṭa names the first-produced sound. The featured point is quarantine: the registry gives the doxographic span its own sense, so the report never contaminates the doctrine. The theorems on both sides stay green.

**Axioms, each with its verbatim license.**

- relation `utpadyate(karana → sphota)`: “the one generated by the articulators' contacts and releases (samyoga-vibhaga”
- relation `sabda_ja(sphota → sabda_ja)`: “the sounds born *from* that sound”
- identity `sabda_ja = dhvani`: “'dhvani' names the śabda-ja sabdah, the sounds born *from* that sound”
- predication `acoustic(sphota)`: “the sphoṭa is itself acoustic and produced”

**Denials.**

- `sphota = partless_sphota`: “the partless meaning-bearer, is not in this picture at all”

**Rejected readings and their compiled fates.**

- “The sphoṭa is the partless, metaphysical word-essence that generates secondary acoustic phenomena.” → **unlicensed**. Verse 1.102 explicitly reports an alternative view that denies this: the sphoṭa here is acoustic and produced, not partless and metaphysical. This reading confuses Bhartṛhari's own doctrine (developed later) with the competing account presented in this verse.
- “The dhvani are the primary sounds generated by the organs; the sphoṭa is a secondary echo or propagation.” → **unlicensed**. Reverses the relation: the verse explicitly states dhvani are sounds *born from* the sphoṭa, making sphoṭa the primary producer. The backward relation is not entailed by the axioms.

**Theorems in `V1_102.lean`.** `accepted_adequate`, `sphota_partless_inadequate`, `dhvani_primary_inadequate`. All close by `decide`; the build carries zero `sorry`.


## The verses

### 1.102

::: {.deva}
यः संयोगविभागाभ्यां करणैरुपजन्यते ।  
स स्फोटः शब्दजाः शब्दा ध्वनयोऽन्यैरुदाहृताः ॥ १०२ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
yaḥ saṃyogavibhāgābhyāṃ karaṇairupajanyate |  
sa sphoṭaḥ śabdajāḥ śabdā dhvanayo'nyairudāhṛtāḥ || 102 ||
:::

> What is generated by the organs through conjunction and disjunction, that is the sphoṭa; the sounds born from that word are what others call the dhvanis.

A terminological about-face, reported deadpan: for *some* theorists (the tradition identifies phonetician-grammarians whose view survives here and in the Mahabhasya's margins), 'sphoṭa' names the *first-produced sound*, the one generated by the articulators' contacts and releases (samyoga-vibhaga, the standard physics of sound-production), while 'dhvani' names the śabda-ja sabdah, the sounds born *from* that sound: the spreading, echoing continuum that carries it to distant ears. On this usage the sphoṭa is itself acoustic and produced; what Bhartṛhari has been calling sphoṭa, the partless meaning-bearer, is not in this picture at all. He reports the usage without protest because half of his own apparatus survives translation into it: the first-sound fixes identity (prākṛta), the propagated train varies freely (vaikṛta, as 1.103 will say in this idiom). But the metaphysical core does not survive, and the sequence 1.102-113 is precisely a tour of what theories look like when the undivided word is left out.

*Contract.* 5 entities · 4 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_102`.

*Refuted.* “The sphoṭa is the partless, metaphysical word-essence that generates secondary acoustic phenomena.” (unlicensed): Verse 1.102 explicitly reports an alternative view that denies this: the sphoṭa here is acoustic and produced, not partless and metaphysical.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* whose view 1.102 reports is uncertain; candidates run from early phoneticians to the audumbarayana-line of speculation, and the karika's anonymous 'anyaih' leaves it open, what is clear is that the terminology here inverts Bhartṛhari's own
:::


### 1.103

::: {.deva}
अल्पे महति वा शब्दे स्फोटकालो न भिद्यते ।  
परस्तु शब्दसन्तानः प्रचयापचयात्मकः ॥ १०३ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
alpe mahati vā śabde sphoṭakālo na bhidyate |  
parastu śabdasantānaḥ pracayāpacayātmakaḥ || 103 ||
:::

> Whether the word is small or great, the time of the sphoṭa does not differ; but the subsequent train of sounds has the nature of increase and decrease.

The produced-sphoṭa theory of 1.102 developing its own version of the invariance thesis. Even with the sphoṭa reduced to the first acoustic event, a distinction imposes itself: that first event's time 'does not differ' with loudness, alpa or mahat, whispered or bellowed, the initial articulated form is what it is, while the para śabda-santana, the after-train, waxes and wanes (pracaya-apacaya) with the force of utterance, which is why a shout carries and a whisper dies. Loudness, on this analysis, is a property of propagation, not of articulation. Bhartṛhari's interest in reporting it is transparent: even theorists who deny the transcendent word are driven to split the invariant from the variable within speech, the structure of prākṛta versus vaikṛta (1.76-77) reappears inside pure acoustics. The rival theory thus testifies, despite itself, that no account of language survives without an invariance-core; the schools differ only over how deep the invariant lies.

*Contract.* 4 entities · 4 axioms · 2 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_103`.

*Refuted.* “The time of the sphoṭa increases and decreases with the loudness of the utterance, while the after-train remains constant.” (contradicted): Reverses the verse's explicit assertion: the sphoṭa-kala does not differ (is invariant), while the śabda-santana varies.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* sphoṭa-kala here must mean the duration of the initially-produced sound if 1.102's terminology is still in force, and we read the verse as continuing that reported view; a reader who takes 1.103 as Bhartṛhari's own voice must instead take it as restating 1.75-76, which the 'para santana' clause fits less well
:::


### 1.104

::: {.deva}
दूरात् प्रभेव दीपस्य ध्वनिमात्रं तु लक्ष्यते ।  
घण्टादीनां च शब्देषु व्यक्तो भेदः स दृश्यते ॥ १०४ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
dūrāt prabheva dīpasya dhvanimātraṃ tu lakṣyate |  
ghaṇṭādīnāṃ ca śabdeṣu vyakto bhedaḥ sa dṛśyate || 104 ||
:::

> From afar, only the sound is discerned, like the glow of a lamp; and in the notes of bells and the like, that difference is plainly seen.

Empirical vindication of the sphoṭa-dhvani split from two everyday observations. First: a voice heard across a valley, one hears *that* someone speaks without hearing *what*: dhvani-matram, bare sound, reaching the ear while the word does not, exactly as a lamp's prabha announces the lamp from a distance at which the flame itself cannot be made out. The manifestor, then, is separable in experience from what it manifests, settling against 1.81's second position (that the dhvani is never itself sensed). Second: the bell. Struck once, it gives its note and then the long after-hum; hearers effortlessly distinguish the strike-sound from its lingering resonance, vyakto bhedah, the difference is patent. That is the alpa/mahat train of 1.103 made audible in a single event: initial form, then propagating tail. Both observations are chosen for being no one's theory: they are data any account must accommodate, and they already contain the distinction the sphoṭa-theorist needs.

*Contract.* 4 entities · 3 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_104`.

*Refuted.* “From afar, the underlying sound cannot be discerned separately, and only the meaning survives the distance; in bells, only the lingering resonance reaches the ear, not the strike.” (contradicted): Asserts that dhvani is imperceptible (avyakta), contradicting the verse's central empirical claim that one hears the bare sound even when the word is not understood.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* which distinction the bell 'plainly shows', strike versus resonance (our reading), or the sphoṭa/dhvani distinction proper carried into non-linguistic sound, turns on how far 1.102's reported terminology still governs; the example works either way, which may be why it was chosen
:::


### 1.105

::: {.deva}
द्रव्याभिघातात् प्रचितौ भिन्नौ दीर्घप्लुतावपि ।  
कम्पे तूपरते जाता नादा वृत्तेर्विशेषकाः ॥ १०५ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
dravyābhighātāt pracitau bhinnau dīrghaplutāvapi |  
kampe tūparate jātā nādā vṛtterviśeṣakāḥ || 105 ||
:::

> From the striking of the substance, in the amplification even the long and prolated arise distinct; but the sounds born when the vibration has ceased are the differentiators of delivery.

The physics pressed one step further, to apportion vowel-quantity itself. Dravya-abhighata, the striking of the sounding body, articulators on air, hammer on bell, yields the pracaya, the amplified initial sound, and within *that*, dirgha and pluta arise as distinct: quantity, being criterial (1.76), belongs to the primary production. But the nādas born kampe uparate, once the vibration has ceased, the after-resonances, the bell's hum, the voice's trail, are vrtter visesakah, differentiators only of delivery: they mark how the utterance decays, never which word it was. The verse thus locks the earlier division (1.76-77: prākṛta bears contrast, vaikṛta bears style) onto an explicit acoustic mechanism: contrastive properties ride the driven phase of the sound, stylistic ones ride its free decay. As physics of vibration this is remarkably observant, driven versus decaying oscillation is a real and fundamental distinction, and as linguistics it completes the assignment of every temporal property of speech to a definite layer of the medium.

*Contract.* 8 entities · 5 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_105`.

*Refuted.* “The sounds that remain after vibration ceases carry the long and prolated vowel distinctions that identify words.” (contradicted): Reverses the acoustic division the commentary insists on: contrastive properties (vowel length) belong to the driven phase, not the free decay.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* pracitau is difficult, we take it as locative of pracaya, 'in the amplification', following 1.103's vocabulary, but it has been construed as dual ('the two accumulated ones') with dirgha-pluta, changing the syntax though not the doctrine
:::


### 1.106

::: {.deva}
अनवस्थितकम्पेऽपि करणे ध्वनयोऽपरे ।  
स्फोटादेवोपजायन्ते ज्वाला ज्वालान्तरादिव ॥ १०६ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
anavasthitakampe'pi karaṇe dhvanayo'pare |  
sphoṭādevopajāyante jvālā jvālāntarādiva || 106 ||
:::

> Even while the vibration of the organ persists unsettled, further sounds are generated from the sphoṭa itself, as flame from another flame.

The propagation mechanism, with a simile that repays pressing. While the karana still trembles, the articulation not yet settled, apare dhvanayah, further sounds, arise sphotad eva: from the first-produced sound itself (1.102's usage), not from fresh strokes of the organ. Each ring of sound begets the next outward ring, jvala jvalantarad iva, as one flame kindles another, the torch passed, not the torch stretched. The model is serial regeneration: sound crosses space by continually re-producing itself in the medium, each phase the cause of its successor. As acoustics, this is the wave-picture in the only idiom available, local excitation propagating by self-replication, the sounding source needed only to seed it. Doctrinally the verse explains how the word reaches far-off hearers without the original articulation reaching them: what arrives is a great-grandchild of the first sound, faithful in form (flame lights flame without loss of fire-nature), degraded only in the free properties, which is why the distant hearer of 1.104 gets dhvani-matra, the form's carrier worn thin.

*Contract.* 5 entities · 3 axioms · 1 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_106`.

*Refuted.* “Further sounds are continuously produced by the unsettled vibration of the organ itself.” (contradicted): Misreads the negation 'not from fresh strokes of the organ' and treats karana as the ongoing source of all sounds.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* sphotad eva here reads naturally only under 1.102's reported terminology (sphoṭa = first-produced sound); a reader taking sphoṭa in Bhartṛhari's own sense must treat the generation as manifestation loosely phrased, and the flame simile fits production better than manifestation, evidence that 1.102-106 form one reported block
:::


### 1.107

::: {.deva}
वायोरणूनां ज्ञानस्य शब्दत्वापत्तिरिष्यते ।  
कैश्चिद्दर्शनभेदो हि प्रवादेष्वनवस्थितः ॥ १०७ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
vāyoraṇūnāṃ jñānasya śabdatvāpattiriṣyate |  
kaiściddarśanabhedo hi pravādeṣvanavasthitaḥ || 107 ||
:::

> By some, it is air, by some the atoms, by some cognition that is held to attain the condition of the word; for the divergence of views among the doctrines is unsettled.

The doxographic frame widens to first principles: what *is* the stuff that 'becomes' word? Three candidate substrates, each with its school: vayu, air, the phonetician's answer, speech as wind shaped (1.108-109 will unfold it); anavah, atoms, an atomist refinement in which sound-particles accumulate (1.110-111); and jñāna, cognition, the idealist answer, word as a modification of awareness itself (1.112-113, where Bhartṛhari's own sympupathies will surface). The second line is a shrug with method in it: darśana-bhedo hi pravadesv anavasthitah, the divergence among doctrines settles nowhere. Bhartṛhari's tolerance is again strategic. His own doctrine lives at a level these disputes do not touch: whatever the substrate-story, the manifested word's undividedness, its transfer-properties, its yogyatā with sounds (1.97) stand unaffected, exactly as 1.28 declined the nitya/kārya fight and 1.74 the part/whole fight. The catalogue that follows is thus not indecision but a demonstration that the sphoṭa doctrine is compatible with every physics on offer, and hostage to none.

*Refuted.* “Air is definitively established as the sole substrate for the word.” (contradicted): Commits to a single substrate view, contradicting the commentary's explicit statement that the divergence of views settles nowhere and Bhartṛhari's demonstration that the sphoṭa doctrine is compatible…


### 1.108-109

::: {.deva}
लब्धक्रियः प्रयत्नेन वक्तुरिच्छानुवर्तिना ।  
स्थानेष्वभिहतो वायुः शब्दत्वं प्रतिपद्यते ॥ १०८ ॥  
तस्य कारणसामर्थ्याद्वेगप्रचयधर्मणः ।  
सन्निपाताद्विभज्यन्ते सारवत्योऽपि मूर्तयः ॥ १०९ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
labdhakriyaḥ prayatnena vakturicchānuvartinā |  
sthāneṣvabhihato vāyuḥ śabdatvaṃ pratipadyate || 108 ||  
tasya kāraṇasāmarthyādvegapracayadharmaṇaḥ |  
sannipātādvibhajyante sāravatyo'pi mūrtayaḥ || 109 ||
:::

> (108) Air, set in motion by the effort that follows the speaker's wish, striking upon the places of articulation, attains the condition of the word. (109) Through the potency of its cause, possessing the properties of velocity and accumulation, it divides by its impact even bodies that possess solidity.

The vayu doctrine given its full statement, and it is a mechanist's credo. The causal chain runs unbroken from mind to matter: iccha, the wish; prayatna anuvartin, effort conforming to it; air labdha-kriya, endowed with motion; abhihata sthanesu, striking the eight classical places of articulation, and the shaped collision *is* the word: sabdatvam pratipadyate, air attains wordhood. Nothing non-physical intervenes between intention and utterance except intention itself. 109 then insists the agent is robust enough for the role: this moving air carries vega and pracaya, velocity and mass-accumulation, from the samarthya of its cause, and at full sannipata it splits even solid bodies, the gale that breaks trees is the same stuff that, finely governed, becomes a vowel. The argument-by-escalation answers the obvious sneer (how can thin breath be the mighty word?) with observed pneumatic violence. Bhartṛhari reports the view fairly and without adoption; its inadequacy for him lies not in the physics but in what the physics cannot reach, air shaped at the lips has still to be *grasped as one word* by a hearer, and the whole dossier of 1.82-92 waits on the far side of any collision.

*Refuted.* “Air alone, shaped by the speaker's will and striking the places of articulation, is fully the word itself, capable of being understood by any hearer.” (contradicted): The verse presents the vayu doctrine as mechanist explanation of word-formation, but the commentary explicitly notes this is incomplete: shaped air must be grasped as one word by the hearer, which req…

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* sāravatyo 'pi mūrtayah, 'even solid bodies', is our construal of saravat as 'possessing pith/solidity'; the line has also been read as 'even the solid forms (of the articulators) are divided', making the wind shape itself by cutting against the organs, which fits the phonetic context more closely though it strains vibhajyante

### 1.110-111

::: {.deva}
अणवः सर्वशक्तित्वाद्भेदसंसर्गवृत्तयः ।  
छायातपतमःशब्दभावेन परिणामिनः ॥ ११० ॥  
स्वशक्तौ व्यज्यमानायां प्रयत्नेन समीरिताः ।  
अभ्राणीव प्रचीयन्ते शब्दाख्याः परमाणवः ॥ १११ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
aṇavaḥ sarvaśaktitvādbhedasaṃsargavṛttayaḥ |  
chāyātapatamaḥśabdabhāvena pariṇāminaḥ || 110 ||  
svaśaktau vyajyamānāyāṃ prayatnena samīritāḥ |  
abhrāṇīva pracīyante śabdākhyāḥ paramāṇavaḥ || 111 ||
:::

> (110) The atoms, because they possess all powers, functioning by division and by combination, transform themselves as shadow, light, darkness and word. (111) When their own power is being manifested, impelled by effort, the atoms called 'word' accumulate like clouds.

The atomist refinement, stated in two strokes. Where the vayu theory made word a *state* of one continuous stuff, this view grinds the substrate finer: anavah, atoms, sarva-saktitvat, omnipotent in the small, each carrying every power, take on word-being as one pariṇāma among siblings: chaya, atapa, tamas, śabda. The company the word keeps in that list is deliberate and strange: shadow, glare, darkness, the quasi-substances of Indian physics, borderline entities that are somehow *there* without ordinary thinghood. Word belongs, for this school, to precisely that twilight ontology. 111 gives the dynamics: under prayatna the śabda-akhyah paramanavah, 'the atoms called word', a name conceding that wordhood is a functional designation, not a natural kind, pracyante abhranive, mass like clouds: scattered potency condensing into audible weather. The cloud-simile earns its place: clouds are aggregates with edges only from a distance, form without unity, which is exactly the standing this theory can give the word, and exactly why it cannot be Bhartṛhari's: a cloud of word-atoms has no principled oneness for the hearer's single grasp (1.84) to seize. The pūrvapakṣa is thus allowed to paint its own limitation.

*Contract.* 9 entities · 5 axioms · 2 denials; adequacy proved in `V1_110_111`.

*Refuted.* “The atoms combine into a single, unified word-substance that hearer can grasp as one.” (unlicensed): The commentary explicitly denies this: clouds are 'aggregates with edges only from a distance, form without unity' and 'a cloud of word-atoms has no principled oneness for the hearer's single grasp'.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* bheda-samsarga-vrttayah we take as 'functioning by division and combination' (the atoms' two modes); it has also been construed as 'whose functioning involves difference-and-contact with one another', a physics of collision, the cosmology is unaffected

### 1.112-113

::: {.deva}
अथायमान्तरो ज्ञाता सूक्ष्मे वागात्मनि स्थितः ।  
व्यक्तये स्वस्य रूपस्य शब्दत्वेन विवर्तते ॥ ११२ ॥  
स मनोभावमापद्य तेजसा पाकमागतः ।  
वायुमाविशति प्राणमथासौ समुदीर्यते ॥ ११३ ॥

::: {.iast}
athāyamāntaro jñātā sūkṣme vāgātmani sthitaḥ |  
vyaktaye svasya rūpasya śabdatvena vivartate || 112 ||  
sa manobhāvamāpadya tejasā pākamāgataḥ |  
vāyumāviśati prāṇamathāsau samudīryate || 113 ||

> (112) Now this inner knower, standing in the subtle self-of-speech, turns itself, for the manifestation of its own form, into wordhood. (113) Attaining the condition of mind, ripened by the inner fire, it enters the breath-wind; and then it is uttered forth.

The third doctrine, and the temperature changes: the anonymous reportage of 1.108-111 gives way to vocabulary that is Bhartṛhari's own. The subject now is the antarah jnata, the inner knower, already standing suksme vagatmani, in a subtle speech-self, language is not something the knower produces but a dimension it inhabits, and the verb is vivartate (112d): it *appears as* wordhood, the same appearance-without-transformation that 1.1 predicated of Brahman and 1.48 of the sphoṭa. And the purpose: vyaktaye svasya rupasya, for the manifestation of its own form, speech as the self's self-disclosure, not as signal-engineering. 113 then supplies what neither rival could: a descent-path connecting this metaphysics to their physics. The word-nature attains mano-bhava (mind-condition), is ripened tejasa, by the inner heat, the old physiological fire of the Upaniṣads, enters prana, and is samudirya, uttered forth. Mind, fire, breath: the idealist keeps the whole pneumatic chain of 1.108 as the *last* stage of a process that begins in awareness. The three-stage descent, knower's subtle speech, mental word, breath-borne utterance, is the embryo of the paśyantī/madhyamā/vaikharī hierarchy made explicit at 1.142, and from here to the kanda's end the reported view and the author's voice are one.

### 1.114

::: {.deva}
अन्तःकरणतत्त्वस्य वायुराश्रयतां गतः ।  
तद्धर्मेण समाविष्टस्तेजसैव विवर्तते ॥ ११४ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
antaḥkaraṇatattvasya vāyurāśrayatāṃ gataḥ |  
taddharmeṇa samāviṣṭastejasaiva vivartate || 114 ||
:::

> The breath, having come to rest upon the principle of the inner organ, pervaded by that principle's properties, appears, by the agency of the fire itself.

The descent of 1.113 examined at its middle joint, where mind and wind interlock. Prana, rising to carry the word out, is not raw air by the time it sounds: it has gone asrayatam, taken the antahkarana-tattva as its resort, and is tad-dharmena samavista, suffused, occupied, by the inner organ's properties. The breath that reaches the lips is mind-conditioned matter, wind already carrying form. And the connective is again tejasa eva vivartate: through the ripening fire, it *appears*, the vivarta verb now applied to the physiological process itself, so that even the pneumatic stage of speech is appearance-of-the-inner rather than fresh production. The verse answers, in passing, the question the vayu-doctrine of 1.108 could not: *why* shaped breath means anything. Because what shapes it from within is the mind's own structure riding down; the wind is expressive because it is invested, not because collisions are eloquent. The mechanist's whole chain is retained and re-read as the outermost garment of self-manifestation.

### 1.115

::: {.deva}
विभजन् स्वात्मनो ग्रन्थीन् श्रुतिरूपैः पृथग्विधैः ।  
प्राणो वर्णानभिव्यज्य वर्णेष्वेवोपलीयते ॥ ११५ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
vibhajan svātmano granthīn śrutirūpaiḥ pṛthagvidhaiḥ |  
prāṇo varṇānabhivyajya varṇeṣvevopalīyate || 115 ||
:::

> Dividing the knots of itself by the differentiated forms of audible sound, the breath, having manifested the phonemes, dissolves into those very phonemes.

The last event of utterance, rendered with a violence and a pathos the doctrine has earned. The breath ascends carrying the undivided word; at the places of articulation it is svatmano granthin vibhajan, dividing the knots of itself. The knots (granthi, the Vṛtti's own word for the involutions of 1.1) are the word's condensed unity as lodged in the moving prana; articulation unties them into śruti-rupaih prthagvidhaih, the differentiated audible forms. Then the extraordinary final clause: the breath, varnan abhivyajya, having manifested the phonemes, varnesv eva upaliyate, dissolves into those very phonemes. The carrier does not survive its delivery; prana spends itself utterly into what it manifests, as if the messenger became the message's letters. This is the production-side answer to 1.54's hearing-side vanishing (words unnoticed, meaning taken): at both ends of speech, the medium consummates itself by disappearing into what it serves. Cosmologically the verse is the microcosm of dissolution-in-manifestation, the one, descending into difference, is wholly *in* the difference, nothing held back, which is why the next verse must immediately insist that the source itself is nonetheless never exhausted.

*Refuted.* “The breath, having manifested the phonemes, remains concealed within those very phonemes.” (contradicted): Contradicts the commentary's core assertion that the carrier does not survive delivery.

::: {.contested}
*Contested.* upaliyate admits both 'dissolves into' (our reading, the breath is used up in the phonemes) and 'merges/hides within' (the breath persists concealed in them); the first suits the physiology, the second the metaphysics of latency, and the word may be chosen to carry both

### 1.116

::: {.deva}
अजस्रवृत्तिर्यः शब्दः सूक्ष्मत्वान्नोपलभ्यते ।  
व्यजनाद्वायुरिव स स्वनिमित्तात् प्रचीयते ॥ ११६ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
ajasravṛttiryaḥ śabdaḥ sūkṣmatvānnopalabhyate |  
vyajanādvāyuriva sa svanimittāt pracīyate || 116 ||
:::

> The word whose functioning is unceasing is not apprehended because of its subtlety; it is made manifest through its own occasioning cause, as air is by a fan.

The insistence 1.115 required: behind each spent utterance stands an unspendable source. The śabda meant here is ajasra-vṛtti, of unceasing operation, the inner word-nature of 1.112, never silent, only suksma, too subtle for apprehension. Its non-appearance is thus an epistemic fact, not an ontological absence, and the simile makes the point with perfect economy: vyajanad vayur iva, as air by a fan. Air is always present, always in some motion; the fan does not create it but pracyate, concentrates and quickens it past the threshold of feeling. So utterance stands to the ever-functioning word: articulation is the fan, a sva-nimitta that raises the perpetual subtle operation to gross perceptibility for a moment, after which the word no more ceases than the air does when the fan stops. The verse retro-illuminates the whole manifestation dossier: this is why abhivyakti and not production was the right category from 1.44 onward, one does not produce what never pauses; one occasions its detection.

*Refuted.* “The utterance produces the word from nothing.” (contradicted): Asserts production (utpatti) of śabda by vyajana.


### 1.117

::: {.deva}
तस्य प्राणे च या शक्तिर्या च बुद्धौ व्यवस्थिता ।  
विवर्तमाना स्थानेषु सैषा भेदं प्रपद्यते ॥ ११७ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
tasya prāṇe ca yā śaktiryā ca buddhau vyavasthitā |  
vivartamānā sthāneṣu saiṣā bhedaṃ prapadyate || 117 ||
:::

> The power of it that stands in the breath, and the power that stands settled in the intellect, that very power, turning itself about in the places of articulation, attains differentiation.

A unification, gathering the descent (1.112-115) into a single subject. What stands in prana and what stands in buddhi are not two agencies but ya ca ... ya ca ... saisa, one śakti doubly stationed, and it is *that* which, vivartamana sthanesu (the vivarta participle again, now in the very throat), bhedam prapadyate, arrives at differentiation. The verse forecloses a dualist misreading the descent-story invited: mind-word and breath-word are not successive entities handed a baton but one power viewed at two stations, its 'motion' from one to the other being appearance, not transit. Read against 1.2, the architecture is exact, as Brahman's many śaktis are apthak, non-separate, from it, so the word-power's stations in intellect and breath are non-separate from the one power. Differentiation happens at the sthanas, the outermost edge, and even there it is something the power *presents*, not something it suffers. Everything since 1.44 is here in one sentence: one word-power, two inner stations, appearance of difference at the threshold of sound.

*Refuted.* “The vital power in the breath and the intellectual power in the intellect are two distinct forces that successively coordinate in producing differentiated speech.” (unlicensed): Treats the two stations as separate powers rather than expressions of one śakti.


### 1.118

::: {.deva}
शब्देष्वेवाश्रिता शक्तिर्विश्वस्यास्य निबन्धनी ।  
यन्नेत्रः प्रतिभात्मायं भेदरूपः प्रतीयते ॥ ११८ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
śabdeṣvevāśritā śaktirviśvasyāsya nibandhanī |  
yannetraḥ pratibhātmāyaṃ bhedarūpaḥ pratīyate || 118 ||
:::

> The power residing in words themselves is what binds this universe; and this self, whose essence is the flash of insight, whose eye is that power, is perceived in the form of difference.

The crescendo before the kanda's final theses, in which the linguistics is folded back into the cosmology without remainder. First line: visvasya asya nibandhani, the śakti lodged sabdesv eva, in words themselves, is the *binder of this universe*. Nibandhana has been this kanda's word for 'ground' since 1.13 (words ground the realities of practice) and 1.15 (word-forms ground the classes of things); now the grounding is total, the world hangs together wordwise, coherence itself being linguistic. Second line: the ātman, pratibhā-atma, whose essence is pratibhā, the immediate flash of understanding, here named for the first time and destined to be the second kanda's answer to what sentence-meaning is, sees *through* that word-power (yan-netra, 'whose eye is it') and accordingly pratiyate bheda-rupah, is perceived in the form of difference. The construction is precise about where difference lives: an undivided seer, seeing through the instrument of the word-power, appears divided *because its eye is the differentiating instrument*, the eye colours the seen, and the world's plurality is the tint of the lens. The verse thus answers 1.1 across the whole arc of the kanda: how does the one Brahman-word appear as the processional world? Through its own power-of-words serving as the very organ of its self-perception.

### 1.119

::: {.deva}
षड्जादिभेदः शब्देन व्याख्यातो रूप्यते यतः ।  
तस्मादर्थविधाः सर्वाः शब्दमात्रासु निश्रिताः ॥ ११९ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
ṣaḍjādibhedaḥ śabdena vyākhyāto rūpyate yataḥ |  
tasmādarthavidhāḥ sarvāḥ śabdamātrāsu niśritāḥ || 119 ||
:::

> Since the differences beginning with the note sadja are explained and given form by word, therefore all the varieties of objects rest upon the measures of word.

An argument from the hardest case: music. The notes of the scale, sadja and its fellows, the seven svaras, are pure audition, the least conceptual of objects, difference given to the ear with no help from thought. And yet, the verse observes, even these are vyakhyata and rupyate sabdena, explained, and *given their form*, through word: the notes are individuated as notes, held apart as a system of seven, only through their names and the theory that names them. The untrained ear hears continuous pitch; the named scale is what carves it into sadja-versus-rsabha. If even audible difference at its most immediate is word-fixed, then a fortiori artha-vidhah sarvah, all the varieties of objects, rest on śabda-matras, the measures of word. The verse converts 1.15's thesis (classes rest on word-forms) from assertion to argument by finding the a fortiori case, and it opens the final ascent: from here to 1.131 the kanda claims everything, cosmos, practice, cognition, salvation, for the word.

### 1.121

::: {.deva}
इतिकर्तव्यता लोके सर्वा शब्दव्यपाश्रया ।  
यां पूर्वाहितसंस्कारो बालोऽपि प्रतिपद्यते ॥ १२१ ॥

:::

> All procedure-knowledge in the world rests upon word, which even the child comprehends, through the residues deposited in it before.

From cosmos to cradle. Itikartavyata, 'the thus-it-is-to-be-done-ness' of things, know-how, the whole practical intelligence by which life is conducted, is sarva śabda-vyapasraya, wholly word-dependent. The counterexample any critic reaches for is the infant: the bala acts purposively, turns to the breast, grasps, avoids, before language. Bhartṛhari's answer is purva-ahita-saṃskāra: the child comprehends through residues laid down before, in prior lives, on the tradition's reckoning; in any case, antecedently to this learning. Infant know-how is not word-free cognition but the operation of word-formed traces not presently audible: an innate endowment that is itself linguistic in structure. The move is only as strong as one's tolerance for its premise, but its shape is exact and modern, practical capacities that precede overt language are explained by an inner, inherited language-likeness rather than admitted as non-linguistic. What 1.123 will call the interweaving is here traced beneath the threshold of speech acquisition.

### 1.122

::: {.deva}
आद्यः करणविन्यासः प्राणस्योर्ध्वं समीरणम् ।  
स्थानानामभिघातश्च न विना शब्दभावनाम् ॥ १२२ ॥
:::

::: {.iast}
ādyaḥ karaṇavinyāsaḥ prāṇasyordhvaṃ samīraṇam |  
sthānānāmabhighātaśca na vinā śabdabhāvanām || 122 ||
:::

> The first disposing of the organ, the driving of the breath upward, the striking upon the places of articulation, none occurs without a conceiving in word.

The dependence turned back on speech itself, completing a circle with deliberate care. Utterance is a motor act: the initial karana-vinyasa (tongue and lips taking position), the upward samirana of breath, the abhighata at the sthanas. Each of these, the verse notes, is itself a piece of itikartavyata, a procedure, and so by 1.121 requires śabda-bhavana, a conceiving or intending in word, to occur at all. One cannot *say* anything without inwardly word-forming the saying of it. The regress this threatens (does the inner wording itself need a prior wording?) is not an oversight but the doctrine: it terminates, non-viciously, in the ajasra-vṛtti word of 1.116, the unceasing inner operation that no act initiates because it never began. Speech does not start from silence; audible speaking is a local intensification (the fan of 1.116) of a wording that is always already under way. The verse thus welds the physiology of 1.108-115 to the epistemology about to be announced: even the body's speech-machinery runs on the very medium it outputs.
