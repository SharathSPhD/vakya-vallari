Formalizing the Infinite: The Intersection of Lean Theorem Proving, Sanskrit Linguistics, and the Verification of Commentarial Traditions
The pursuit of formalizing human language and reasoning spans millennia, bridging the ancient grammatical traditions of the Indian subcontinent with contemporary advancements in computational logic and type theory. At the nexus of this historical continuum lies an unprecedented opportunity: the application of modern mechanized reasoning—specifically interactive theorem provers like Lean and Coq—to evaluate, verify, and validate the translation and interpretation of classical Sanskrit texts. Sanskrit, distinguished by its mathematical precision, highly inflectional morphology, and rule-based generative architecture, presents unique challenges and opportunities for natural language processing, semantic representation, and formal verification1.
The classical Indian philosophical tradition relies heavily on an expansive commentarial literature to unpack the extreme semantic density of foundational aphorisms. The central inquiry of this comprehensive report investigates how modern theorem provers, alongside neuro-symbolic autoformalization frameworks, can be leveraged to mathematically verify the robustness of Sanskrit translations. The goal is to ensure that target-language outputs strictly adhere to the logical, ontological, and epistemic constraints codified in classical commentaries, such as those accompanying Bhartṛhari’s seminal text on the philosophy of language, the Vākyapadīya. By establishing a continuous theoretical lineage from Pāṇinian grammar and Navya-Nyāya logic to Cubical Type Theory and the Grammatical Framework (GF), this analysis constructs a novel methodology for the formal verification of natural language semantics.
The Computational Lineage of Sanskrit: Pāṇinian Grammar as an Algorithmic Precursor
To comprehend the feasibility of applying formal verification to Sanskrit translation, one must first examine the inherent computational architecture of the language itself. Pāṇini’s Aṣṭādhyāyī, composed around the 6th to 4th century BCE, is universally recognized not merely as a descriptive grammar, but as a formal, generative rule-based system that anticipates the theoretical foundations of modern computer science by over two millennia1. The treatise sums up the science of phonetics and morphology in approximately 4,000 succinct aphorisms, divided into eight chapters, establishing a system likened to a Turing machine in its use of metarules, transformations, and recursive logic6.
The generative engine of the Aṣṭādhyāyī operates as a highly sophisticated rewrite mechanism, transforming abstract linguistic intentions into surface phonetic forms1. Pāṇini established a technical metalanguage to define the rules governing the object language without requiring a separate grammar for the metalanguage itself—a self-hosting paradigm unique even among modern programming languages4. This metalanguage employs auxiliary markers, known as anubandhas or it markers (such as śit, mit, ṅit), that function as control tokens directing the application of specific morphological transformations. This concept is directly analogous to the non-terminals and auxiliary markers used in Emil Post’s rewrite systems and modern compiler design, allowing the grammar to govern word formation, affixation, and syntactic derivation through recursive operations2.
The structural flow of Pāṇinian derivation operates deterministically, utilizing a hierarchical arrangement of rules. The system relies heavily on foundational alphabetic organization known as the Śiva-sūtras (or Maheśvara-sūtras), a set of fourteen aphorisms that meticulously order the phonemes of the Sanskrit language to facilitate the creation of pratyāhāras7. A pratyāhāra is an algorithmic abbreviation used to refer to a specific class of sounds. For instance, the pratyāhāra aC refers to all vowels, while haL refers to all consonants, allowing Pāṇini to write highly compressed rules that apply to vast categories of phonemes simultaneously4.
A persistent challenge in modeling the Aṣṭādhyāyī computationally has been the resolution of rule conflicts—determining which rule takes precedence when multiple transformations apply to a single linguistic state. Classical tradition relies on the concepts of utsarga (general rules) and apavāda (exceptions), where more specific exception rules override general rules11. Recent algorithmic decodings of Pāṇini's metarules have demonstrated that the system is entirely deterministic, utilizing spatial conflict resolution where rules applicable to the right-side operand in a derivation typically take precedence, rendering the system capable of generating millions of grammatically correct words through a machine-executable step-by-step process8.

	The Kāraka Framework: Bridging Syntax and Semantic Representation
The interface between syntax and semantics in Sanskrit is governed by the Kāraka theory, a structural paradigm that dictates how actions relate to their participants. Kāraka refers to the semantico-syntactic relations between nominals (nouns or pronouns) and the central action (verb or kriyā) of a sentence16. Unlike traditional Western grammatical cases, which are largely structural, Kārakas denote the ontological participant roles in an action, establishing a direct link between linguistic expression and cognitive reality18.
Because Sanskrit is a highly inflected language with free word order, the semantic payload of a sentence is carried entirely by morphological markers (suffixes and declensions) rather than the physical positioning of the words3. This allows the Kāraka system to function as a robust, native knowledge representation framework, mapping cleanly onto conceptual graphs and semantic networks utilized in artificial intelligence and dependency parsing16. By treating the verb as the central root node and the Kārakas as directed edges connecting to participant entities, the theory transforms natural language parsing into the generation of precise, unambiguous dependency trees20.

For example, defining the karman as the entity "most desired by the agent" (karturīpsitatamam) or the karaṇa as the "most effective" (sādhakatamam) introduces subjective, semantic conditions that are difficult to formalize in pure syntactic code15. Consequently, automated verification of a translation fundamentally requires establishing a semantic isomorphism between the source Kāraka graph and the target language's logical form, bridging the gap between mere grammatical parsing and true semantic understanding17.
Semantic Holism and the Ontological Architectures of the Vākyapadīya
While Pāṇini provided the structural mechanics of Sanskrit, the philosophical and semantic foundations were most rigorously articulated by the 5th-century philosopher-grammarian Bhartṛhari in his magnum opus, the Vākyapadīya (Treatise on Sentences and Words)22. Comprising approximately 635 verses divided into three distinct books (the Brahma-kāṇḍa, Vākya-kāṇḍa, and Pada-kāṇḍa), the text represents a monumental shift in Indian intellectual history23. Bhartṛhari moved linguistic inquiry beyond mechanical grammatical analysis to engage deeply with epistemology, cognitive science, and metaphysics, asserting that the study of grammar is a path to spiritual liberation and the ultimate comprehension of reality23.
At the core of the Vākyapadīya is the Sphoṭa theory, which addresses the fundamental paradox of sequential language: how does a linear sequence of transient phonetic sounds produce a unified, instantaneous cognition of meaning? Bhartṛhari posited that individual phonemes (varṇa) and words (pada) have no independent ontological reality; they are merely pedagogical fictions created to explain language to the uninitiated26. The true, indivisible unit of communication is the sentence as a unified whole (vākya-sphoṭa).
When a speaker formulates a thought, the meaning exists in a pre-verbal, sequenceless state of pure consciousness (paśyantī vāk). It is then translated into a sequenced but unvocalized mental schema (madhyamā) before finally being articulated as sequential physical sounds (vaikharī)24. The listener processes this sequential acoustic data, which triggers an intuitive, instantaneous burst of comprehension (pratibhā) that reconstructs the unified meaning, much like a single light illuminating a complex painting26. This structuralist view of language—positing an underlying unity beneath superficial difference (abhedapūrvako hi bhedaḥ)—strongly parallels the abstraction mechanisms in modern type theory and lambda calculus, where complex, sequentially evaluated computational expressions ultimately reduce to a single, unified canonical form or type26.
The Indian epistemic tradition views verbal testimony (śabda) as a valid means of knowledge (pramāṇa). The intricate cognitive process by which a hearer derives this knowledge is termed śābdabodha (verbal cognition)34. Successful śābdabodha requires three stringent linguistic conditions to be met simultaneously: ākāṅkṣā (syntactic expectancy, where words demand the presence of other words to complete a thought), yogyatā (semantic compatibility and non-contradiction between the linked entities), and sannidhi (the spatio-temporal contiguity of the utterance)35.
When interpreting complex classical texts, a reader often encounters "cognitive friction" (jñāna-gharṣaṇa)—situations where the initial, literal interpretation of a word lacks yogyatā with the rest of the sentence. This incompatibility forces the intellect to backtrack, traverse the lexical graph, and select a secondary or figurative meaning (lakṣaṇā) that restores semantic harmony to the entire proposition36.
Bhartṛhari's exploration of these cognitive concepts extends profoundly into ontology. In the Brahmakāṇḍa, he defines the Absolute Reality (Brahman) as the ultimate Word-Principle (Śabda-Brahman), asserting that all phenomenal existence is merely a manifestation (vivarta) of language driven by the dynamic power of Time (Kāla-śakti)24. Time acts as the efficient cause that imposes sequence upon the sequenceless Absolute, generating the illusion of past, present, and future through the dual forces of permission (abhyanuja) and prevention (pratibandha)29. Consequently, any computational framework attempting to verify the translation of texts like the Vākyapadīya must possess the expressive bandwidth to model highly abstract ontological dependencies, sequential temporal reasoning, and the dynamic resolution of semantic ambiguities.
The Epistemological Function of Commentarial Traditions (Bhāṣya)
To navigate the extreme compression and ontological density of texts like the Aṣṭādhyāyī and the Vākyapadīya, the Indian intellectual tradition relies on a vast, continuous ecosystem of commentaries. A commentary (bhāṣya, vṛtti, or ṭīkā) acts as a logical decompiler for the root text40. Because Sanskrit sūtras are designed to be memorized, they are aggressively compressed, often omitting verbs and relying on context to supply meaning. The commentator provides a natural prose reading (anvaya), supplies missing verbal copulas, explicates hidden Kāraka relations, and resolves cognitive friction by specifying the precise contextual meaning of polysemous terms36.
More importantly, a commentary defines the absolute logical and epistemic constraints of the text. It utilizes structured reasoning mechanisms—such as addressing hypothetical objections (pūrvapakṣa) and establishing verified conclusions (siddhānta)—to demonstrate why alternative interpretations are logically invalid42. Commentaries weave disjointed aphorisms into a coherent philosophical tapestry by establishing saṅgati (causal, evidential, or explanatory relationships) between sections40. The tradition categorizes saṅgati into six distinct logical bridges: ākṣepa (objection), dṛṣṭānta (illustration), prati-dṛṣṭānta (counter-illustration), prasaṅga (incidental illustration), utpatti (introduction), and apavāda (exception)41.
In the rigorous terms of Navya-Nyāya logic, the commentary prevents definitions from suffering catastrophic logical faults. A valid definition must be completely free from three specific errors: under-pervasion (avyāpti, where the definition applies to only some instances of the target object), over-pervasion (ativyāpti, where the definition erroneously applies to objects outside the target class), and absolute absurdity (asambhava, where the definition applies to no existing case)42.
Therefore, a "robust" translation of a Sanskrit text is not merely one that is grammatically permissible; it is one that perfectly aligns with the semantic and ontological boundaries established by a specific commentary. Any computational verification of a translation must, therefore, encode the commentary not as supplementary text, but as a rigid set of axiomatic constraints against which the target translation is evaluated.
Formalizing Indian Logic: From Navya-Nyāya to Cubical Type Theory
The necessity of resolving semantic ambiguity in philosophical discourse led to the development of Navya-Nyāya (the "New Logic" school) in the 14th century, spearheaded by scholars such as Gaṅgeśa Upādhyāya and Raghunātha Śiromaṇi40. Recognizing that natural language is fraught with imprecision, these scholars engineered a hyper-precise technical sub-language of Sanskrit to serve as a self-presenting precision logic, avoiding the need to invent a detached, content-independent symbolic notation45.
Navya-Nyāya takes relations (sambandha), rather than predicative auxiliaries, as ontologically primary. The framework relies heavily on concepts such as the delimitor (avacchedaka), which strictly defines and restricts the scope of a property or relation; typed absence (abhāva), which treats non-existence as a structured, positive entity requiring exact coordinates; and identity (tādātmya)45.
Historically, attempts to formalize Navya-Nyāya using First-Order Logic (FOL) or classical higher-order logic have systematically failed. These Western systems lose critical, load-bearing structures because they cannot adequately represent unbounded relational depth (paramparā-sambandha), where a relation itself acts as a relatum for further relations, nor can they handle the nuanced epistemology of typed absence45.
A monumental paradigm shift has occurred with the recent encoding of Navya-Nyāya into CCHM De Morgan Cubical Type Theory (CTT)45. Dependent Type Theory, heavily utilized in modern proof assistants like Coq and Lean, provides the exact mathematical structures required to map Indian precision logic natively into executable code:

This maps directly to the De Morgan involution within CTT, allowing the ontological doctrine to be computed natively as a definitional equality within the theorem prover45.
Similarly, non-classical Indian logic systems, such as the Buddhist Catuṣkoṭi (the four-cornered tetralemma), have been successfully formalized using the Lean theorem prover. In a notable proof of concept, researcher Stephen Diehl utilized Lean's inductive types to map the four states of the Catuṣkoṭi: It is (T), It is not (F), It is and is not (B), and It is neither nor is not (N)48. By defining a custom BState type and a specific negation function bNeg (¬ᵇ), Lean was able to mechanically verify complex properties, such as double negation, across a bounded, distributive lattice that defies standard Boolean logic48. These breakthroughs demonstrate unequivocally that modern type-theoretic proof assistants possess the requisite expressive bandwidth to formalize the most complex ontological and logical frameworks of the Sanskrit tradition.
Interactive Theorem Provers and Autoformalization Frameworks
To leverage the formalisms of Sanskrit grammar and Navya-Nyāya for translation verification, one must employ interactive theorem provers. Systems like Coq (now Rocq) and Lean 4 operate on the Curry-Howard correspondence, a profound mathematical theorem establishing a direct isomorphism between computer programs and mathematical proofs: types represent logical propositions, and the functional programs that compute them represent the proofs of those propositions49.
Lean 4, developed primarily by Leonardo de Moura at Microsoft Research and AWS, serves as both a purely functional programming language and a highly advanced proof assistant based on the calculus of inductive constructions51. It enforces the de Bruijn criterion, meaning all complex proofs generated by high-level tactics or artificial intelligence must ultimately compile down to simple, primitive steps that are independently verified by a small, highly trusted kernel50. Lean has gained immense traction in the mathematical community due to its robust library ecosystem (Mathlib) and its capacity to mechanize complex abstractions, culminating in its use for verifying major mathematical theorems and software infrastructure51.
The challenge of interfacing fluid natural language with rigid theorem provers has birthed the rapidly advancing field of autoformalization—the automated conversion of human-written text into machine-verifiable formal logic52. A pioneering architecture in this domain is GFLean, an open-source framework that utilizes the Grammatical Framework (GF) in conjunction with Haskell to translate controlled natural language directly into Lean code54.
The GFLean pipeline operates as a highly structured, multi-stage compilation process. First, the Grammatical Framework acts as a high-level tool for defining multilingual grammars by separating Abstract Syntax (the underlying logical structure of the text) from Concrete Syntax (the language-specific linearization rules)55. When a natural language sentence is parsed, GF generates a language-agnostic Abstract Syntax Tree (AST)55.
This raw AST is then processed by a Haskell backend to resolve linguistic ambiguities and normalize entities. The simplification step involves assigning unique identifiers to unnamed variables, unifying variable references, and converting embedded quantifiers from in-situ to ex-situ logical positions55. During this phase, lexical items are systematically mapped to Lean types: terms classified as rawNoun0 (e.g., "integer") become Lean types (Int), rawNoun2 terms (e.g., "exponent") become binary functions, and rawAdjective0 terms become unary relations52. Finally, a secondary GF grammar is utilized to linearize the transformed AST directly into syntax that the Lean kernel can process and verify52.
While early iterations of autoformalization relied on strict, rule-based grammars, the state-of-the-art involves neuro-symbolic integration. Large Language Models (LLMs) are now employed to parse highly ambiguous natural language into intermediate Knowledge Equations (KEs) or to draft preliminary Lean code. If the generated code fails to type-check, the formal error trace from the Lean kernel is fed back into the LLM as a prompt for iterative repair—an agentic workflow that brilliantly blends neural pattern recognition with symbolic mathematical rigor58.
Translation Validation: Extending Code Verification to Natural Semantics
The conceptual mechanism required to verify a translation originates not in linguistics, but in compiler design. Translation validation is a formal methodology used to ensure that a compiler’s optimization pass has not inadvertently altered the core semantics of the source code. Instead of undertaking the monumental, often computationally intractable task of verifying the entire compiler software itself (as seen in the CompCert project), translation validation employs a lightweight, independent validator function to check, a posteriori, that the output target code behaves identically to the input source code62.
In a verified compiler pipeline, the validator models the transformation as a mathematical function. If the validator outputs true, it guarantees formal semantic equivalence, rigorously checking for memory state consistency, return value equivalence, and the strict absence of newly introduced undefined behaviors62.
By abstracting this paradigm, computer scientists and linguists are now applying translation validation directly to natural language59. In this innovative context, "compiling" refers to the translation of a text from a source human language (e.g., Sanskrit) to a target human language (e.g., English). The goal of Natural Language Translation Validation (NL-TV) is to mathematically prove that the logical propositions and dependencies entailed by the target text are formally, logically equivalent to those of the source text59. When a translator modifies a sentence to sound more natural in English, they are effectively running an "optimization pass" on the text. NL-TV ensures this linguistic optimization has not corrupted the original meaning.
The Commentary-Driven Translation Validation (C-TV) Methodology
The synthesis of Pāṇinian grammar, Kāraka semantic extraction, Navya-Nyāya logic encoding, Lean autoformalization, and translation validation establishes the theoretical bedrock for a groundbreaking application: mathematically verifying the robustness of Sanskrit translations and their strict adherence to traditional commentaries.
Translating classical Sanskrit, particularly profoundly dense philosophical texts like the Vākyapadīya, is notoriously difficult. The pervasive use of sandhi (euphonic combinations at word boundaries) obscures tokenization, creating a massive computational search space for possible phonetic splits2. Furthermore, nominal compounding (samāsa) enables the construction of extensive, highly compressed semantic units where the relational links between members are entirely implicit and unwritten14. Consequently, English translations of Sanskrit verses often experience a massive token expansion—frequently a 20x increase in length—as the translator is forced to unpack the embedded morphology and insert auxiliary syntax to render the meaning intelligible to a modern reader68.
During this expansion, semantic ambiguity runs rampant. A single sequence of syllables may yield entirely different logical propositions depending on how the compounds are resolved. To mathematically verify that a translation is accurate, we propose the Commentary-Driven Translation Validation (C-TV) framework. This neuro-symbolic pipeline operates through a rigorous, multi-stage compilation and verification process:
Stage 1: Formalizing the Source Text and Commentary
The source Sanskrit verse is first processed using a deterministic Pāṇinian parser. Advanced computational tools utilizing the Aṣṭādhyāyī engine resolve the sandhi, generate precise morphological tags, and identify compound structures, effectively converting the surface text into a machine-readable format12. Following this, the parser extracts the dependency graph based on the Kāraka theory, mapping the syntactic cases (vibhaktis) directly to their underlying semantic roles15. The Grammatical Framework (GF) then converts this Sanskrit dependency graph into a language-agnostic Abstract Syntax Tree (AST), representing the Intermediate Logical Form (ILF) of the text21.
Crucially, the corresponding traditional commentary (bhāṣya or vṛtti) is simultaneously parsed and autoformalized into Lean 4 using the Cubical Type Theory encoding of Navya-Nyāya45. The commentary acts as a strict set of logical axioms and type constraints appended to the ILF. For example, if the root text uses an ambiguous term, the formalized commentary restricts the type signature of that term, defining its exact delimitor (avacchedaka) and specific relation (sambandha).
Stage 2: Autoformalizing the Target Translation
The human-generated English (or Hindi) translation of the verse is ingested by the system. Using an agentic autoformalization workflow similar to GFLean and LLM-driven logical translation, the English text is converted into its underlying formal logic representation, creating the Target Logical Form (TLF)55. During this phase, the autoformalization agent systematically assigns identifiers, handles quantifier scopes, resolves anaphora, and generates the corresponding Lean expressions that represent the English translator's intended meaning52.
Stage 3: Semantic Verification and Agentic Repair in Lean
The crux of the framework lies in the verification phase. The Lean 4 kernel is tasked with proving a theorem of semantic equivalence, effectively functioning as the translation validator. Lean must prove that the Intermediate Logical Form (the source text) is semantically equivalent to the Target Logical Form (the translation), strictly bounded by the constraints (  ) of the formalized commentary59.
Lean first performs rigorous Type Compatibility Checking64. It verifies that the entities defined in the target translation are type-compatible with the source ontology. If the English translation assigns a property to a noun that the Sanskrit commentary explicitly defines as an independent substance (dravya), Lean's type checker will flag an immediate incompatibility. Lean then attempts to construct a mathematical proof that the propositions asserted by the English translation logically follow from the Sanskrit root text. If the English translator has injected a foreign ontological concept—or failed to capture a nested dependency explicitly required by the commentary—the proof will fail to compile50.
If the verification fails, an LLM agent analyzes Lean's error trace, adjusts the autoformalization of the English translation to correct minor syntactic misinterpretations, and attempts the proof again. If the failure persists after agentic repair, the framework outputs a localized error report, indicating exactly where the human translation logically deviated from the commentarial tradition, thus preventing the errors of avyāpti or ativyāpti59.
Application: Verifying the Vākyapadīya
To vividly illustrate this architecture in practice, consider the challenge of verifying a translation of the opening verse of Bhartṛhari’s Vākyapadīya. In the Brahmakāṇḍa, Bhartṛhari states: anādinidhanaṃ brahma śabdatattvaṃ yad akṣaram.
A naive, structurally valid but philosophically hollow translation might read: "The imperishable Brahman, which is the essence of words, has no beginning or end."
However, the commentarial tradition (e.g., the Vṛtti or Hari Vṛṣabha's commentary) establishes rigorous ontological constraints on these precise terms. The commentary specifies that śabdatattva (word-principle) is not merely a linguistic essence or grammatical rule, but the very ontological ground of existence from which the phenomenal world (vivarta) manifests through the dynamic power of Time (kāla)24.
If an English translator renders śabdatattva simply as "linguistic structure," the autoformalized Target Logical Form (TLF) will type it as a linguistic property or characteristic. The C-TV framework, loaded with the formalized commentary, will possess an axiom stating that śabdatattva is equivalent to Brahman (an independent, supreme ontological Absolute)24. When the Lean kernel attempts to unify the TLF (where śabdatattva equals a linguistic property) with the ILF constraints (where śabdatattva equals an Absolute Entity), the type-checking will immediately fail. The Lean kernel will mathematically prove that the English translation is not robust with respect to the authoritative commentary, as it has fundamentally altered the ontology of the text.
Conversely, if the translation captures the exact ontological equivalence and dependency structures dictated by the commentary, Lean will successfully generate a verified proof object. This provides a mathematically certified guarantee that the translation preserves the exact philosophical semantics intended by the classical tradition, free from modern interpretive drift, linguistic dilution, or the subjective biases of the translator.
Broader Implications and Future Trajectories
The implications of establishing a formalized pipeline linking Sanskrit linguistics, commentary validation, and Lean theorem proving extend far beyond classical Indology. This convergence catalyzes profound advancements across multiple domains of artificial intelligence, cognitive science, and software verification.
Current Large Language Models exhibit remarkable capabilities in natural language generation but suffer persistently from logical hallucination and a lack of formal grounding60. The C-TV framework provides a mechanism to ground LLM translations in verifiable, symbolic truth. By treating natural language generation as a formal synthesis problem checked by a kernel like Lean, researchers can force LLMs to adhere strictly to complex, multi-layered constraints55. The highly structured nature of Sanskrit grammar and Navya-Nyāya logic provides the ideal stress-test for these neuro-symbolic systems, pushing the boundaries of what automated reasoners can comprehend regarding deep semantic nuance and unbounded relational depth2.
Furthermore, the successful mapping of Pāṇinian grammar and Kāraka theory onto type-theoretic Abstract Syntax Trees validates the premise that certain linguistic structures share a fundamental mathematical architecture. As researchers encode the Aṣṭādhyāyī into parsers capable of interfacing with tools like the Grammatical Framework, it becomes possible to design language-agnostic metagrammars74. This facilitates the creation of a unified computational foundation for processing all modern Indic languages (which inherit Pāṇinian structures), massively reducing the need for fragmented, language-specific training data and advancing global NLP equity3.
From a cultural and epistemological standpoint, the translation of ancient texts into Western languages often suffers from the unconscious imposition of foreign conceptual frameworks76. Western logical paradigms have historically struggled to accurately represent non-dualistic (advaita) philosophies, theories of sequenceless time, or the intricacies of Indian formal logic45. By formalizing these texts natively in Cubical Type Theory—which can mathematically accommodate concepts like the indiscernibility of indiscernibles and non-extensional identity without losing computational decidability—we preserve the authentic epistemic integrity of the tradition45. The theorem prover becomes a neutral, mathematical arbiter, ensuring that the profound wisdom of antiquity is transmitted into the digital age with absolute, verifiable fidelity.
The intersection of Lean theorem proving and the Sanskrit grammatical tradition represents a profound full-circle moment in the history of logic. Over two millennia ago, Pāṇini devised the world's first formal, generative language system. Centuries later, Navya-Nyāya scholars engineered a precision logic capable of unbounded relational mapping. Today, these ancient innovations find their perfect technological counterpart in Dependent Type Theory and interactive proof assistants. Through frameworks like Commentary-Driven Translation Validation, we elevate natural language translation from an art of subjective interpretation to a rigorous science of verifiable truth.
